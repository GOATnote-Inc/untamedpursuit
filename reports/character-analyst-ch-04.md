## Voice Distinctiveness

For each speaking character:

- **Samuel Taylor**:
  - Voice consistent with profile? Yes
  - Speech pattern match: Candid, self-deprecating humor ("It has ambitions of returning to its original color"), comfortable with silence, says what he means without ornamentation ("I think it is perfectly suited to us"). Philosophical when the moment warrants it ("I believe that art and music can help people access parts of themselves that they would not be able to otherwise") but never performatively so. His dialogue is economical -- he does not fill air.
  - Could this dialogue be swapped with another character? No. Samuel's mix of directness, self-aware humor, and emotional honesty is distinct from Clara's measured layering, Thomas's wit-as-performance, or Harper's professional restraint.

- **Clara Chen**:
  - Voice consistent with profile? Yes
  - Speech pattern match: Measured, deliberate, layered. "Thank you" cuts off Samuel's compliment before he delivers it -- silk and steel. "Enjoy the art, Mr. Taylor. I will see you after" -- controlled, anticipating, a directive wrapped in a pleasantry. "I may have arranged certain aspects" -- contained amusement, the register for a design that landed. "Your tears -- those I did not expect" -- brutally honest, disarmingly vulnerable, the sudden drop into candor that her profile describes.
  - Could this dialogue be swapped with another character? No. Clara's surface/subtext layering and her precision in choosing when to be direct are uniquely hers.

- **Lily Montgomery**:
  - Voice consistent with profile? Yes, with a note.
  - Speech pattern match: "We notice many things. Though perhaps not as many as Clara" -- warm, observant, carrying affection for Clara in the understatement. Brief, certain handshake. However, the line "I understand you've been studying our gallery. The ocean painting caught your attention" (lines 142-143) is more informed and pointed than her profile's "observes before she speaks" pattern would naturally produce, especially given the unattributed knowledge problem (see Experiential Grounding).
  - Could this dialogue be swapped with another character? No. Lily's warmth and her deference to Clara's greater perceptiveness are specific to her.

- **Harper**:
  - Voice consistent with profile? Yes
  - Speech pattern match: "Miss Chen will receive visitors in the private gallery. If you'll follow me." -- professional, restrained, formal address. Actions (flowers, guidance) over words. His silence communicates more than speech.
  - Could this dialogue be swapped with another character? No. Harper's professional brevity is unmistakable.

## Internal Contradictions (Positive)

- **Samuel weeps openly at music while carrying the marks of violence**: The bruised jaw and aching ribs (from a dock fight) coexist with tears during a cello performance. He "had never seen the purpose in hiding what music did to him" -- a man who trains nightly with a weapons master and does not wipe his face when beauty moves him. This is the central contradiction of his profile ("a man of violence drawn to beauty") rendered in a single scene.

- **Samuel's attempt to impress Clara with "his" discovery backfires into deeper admiration**: He brings Clara to the Golden Compass to show her art and music he loves, not knowing she created and owns the entire venue. His response to this discovery is not embarrassment but deeper fascination -- he reads the curation as another expression of her intelligence. His ego is subordinate to his curiosity, which is itself a contradiction for a man of his power and wealth.

- **George's dual register**: The same animal who "broke a man's neck" now settles his head in Samuel's lap "with unhurried tenderness." The chapter renders this without apology or explanation -- the two capacities coexist as naturally as breathing. This mirrors Clara's own duality and Samuel's.

- **Clara's orchestration vs. genuine vulnerability**: Clara controlled every aspect of the evening -- the venue, the flowers, the timing, George's placement, the dining room. The one thing she could not orchestrate was Samuel's tears, and it is precisely his uncontrolled response that cracks her composure. "Your tears -- those I did not expect." The master planner is most moved by what she could not plan.

## Experiential Grounding

This is the critical section. For every significant emotional reaction, memory, or psychological insight in Samuel's interiority, I trace the claimed experience to its on-page source.

### 1. Samuel remembers Clara killing twelve men

- **Claimed experience** (line 26): "the woman who had killed twelve men to save his life"
- **On-page source**: Ch-01, lines 74-96. Samuel was present for the entire fight. He witnessed Clara's shooting, blade work, and George's attacks.
- **Match**: Partially. The text of ch-01 establishes twelve men down total, but George killed at least one (broke a man's neck, line 88) and Samuel himself retrieved a pistol (line 92). Samuel attributing all twelve kills to Clara is an overstatement.
- **Verdict**: GROUNDED (as subjective memory). Samuel was concussed and beaten during the fight. In ch-01 (line 134), he himself said "You also just killed twelve or more men in what seemed to be a blur." His memory attributes all kills to Clara because that is how he experienced it -- a blur of her capability. The continuity checker already flagged this as a defensible subjective exaggeration. The emotional reasoning (awe at Clara) is built on real experience, even if the specific number is imprecise.

### 2. Samuel recalls the tea at Chestnut Street and the handshake

- **Claimed experience** (line 40): "He had been thinking about this evening since the handshake at the Chestnut Street parlor -- the warm grip, the steady eyes, the sense of something opening that he wanted to walk toward."
- **On-page source**: Ch-03, lines 151-157. Samuel and Clara shook hands at the end of the tea. "His grip was firm despite the bruises, calloused, warm. They held the handshake for a beat longer than commerce required."
- **Match**: Yes. "Warm grip" matches "calloused, warm." "Steady eyes" matches the ch-03 descriptions of Clara's direct gaze throughout the tea scene. "Something opening" is Samuel's emotional interpretation, consistent with the ch-03 ending: "not yet trust, but the ground where trust could grow."
- **Verdict**: GROUNDED. The memory matches the source chapter. The emotional coloring (anticipation, a sense of something opening) is Samuel's inference from his own experience, not information he lacks.

## Flags

- **Voice overlap**: None detected. All five speaking characters (Samuel, Clara, Harper, Lily, George via behavior) are distinctly voiced. Samuel's self-deprecating candor, Clara's layered precision, Harper's professional brevity, and Lily's warm observance are all differentiated.

- **Unmotivated action**: None detected. Every action in the chapter serves an established motivation.

- **Missing interiority**: None. Samuel's inner life is richly rendered -- his physical pain, his aesthetic response to art, his reading of Clara, his tears, his gratitude for George's comfort, his recognition of the evening's orchestration. This is one of the most interiorly rich chapters in the book so far.

- **Arc stagnation**: None. Samuel enters the chapter as a man who knows Clara as a warrior and businesswoman. He exits knowing her as an artist, curator, and performer. The arc advances meaningfully.

- **Ungrounded interiority**: Two items require attention:

  1. **Lily's knowledge of Samuel's gallery tour (ERROR)**: Lines 142-143. Lily says "I understand you've been studying our gallery. The ocean painting caught your attention." Lily was backstage and then performing during Samuel's gallery tour. No on-page source shows anyone telling her about his behavior. While Clara or Harper could have relayed this information, that transfer is not shown or referenced. This is unattributed knowledge -- a character acting on information she has no demonstrated access to. This is the most significant experiential grounding failure in the chapter. **Recommended fix**: Add a brief attribution -- e.g., "Clara tells me you've been studying our gallery" or a narrative beat noting that Clara mentioned Samuel's interests to Lily.

  2. **Golden Compass discovery story conflicts with bible (ERROR)**: Line 52. The chapter says Samuel "found his way in three months ago through a business associate who knew his taste. He had walked past the building twice before that introduction." The bible says he was "drawn by a cello through an open window." These are mutually exclusive discovery mechanisms. Since ch-04 is the source chapter and the bible's version appears to derive from earlier source material (Golden_Compass_Stage_Episode) rather than the drafted chapter text, one version must be chosen. This is not strictly an experiential grounding issue (both versions are Samuel's own memory), but it is a bible/draft contradiction that affects the reliability of Samuel's interiority. **Recommendation**: Decide which version is canonical. If the business-associate version (current draft) is chosen, update the bible. If the cello-through-window version (current bible) is chosen, revise lines 52-53 of the chapter.

  3. **Ocean painting in two locations (SPATIAL, not experiential)**: Already flagged by the continuity checker. The ocean painting appears in the public gallery (line 64) and then in the private gallery (line 132) during the same evening. This is a spatial continuity issue rather than an experiential grounding issue per se, but it creates a dissonance in Samuel's perception -- he cannot logically see the same painting in two places during one visit. The narrative attempts to resolve this with "the public display merely a loan from this more intimate room," but this does not explain how the painting moved mid-evening.

## Summary

Chapter 4 is a strong character chapter. Samuel's voice is distinct and consistent with his profile. His emotional responses -- the tears, the awe, the humor, the growing attraction -- are grounded in experiences the reader has witnessed alongside him. The relationship dynamics (Samuel-Clara, Samuel-George, Samuel-Harper, Clara-Lily) all match their bible entries and advance naturally.

The experiential grounding is largely excellent. Of the 15 significant interiority moments traced, 14 are fully grounded in on-page sources. Samuel's memories of ch-01 and ch-03 match what actually happened in those chapters (with the expected minor compression of memory, such as "twelve men" attributed solely to Clara and "a child" instead of "two girls," both defensible as subjective POV).

The two substantive issues are:
1. **Lily's unattributed knowledge** (lines 142-143) -- the most significant experiential grounding error, requiring a simple attribution fix.
2. **The Golden Compass discovery discrepancy** (line 52) -- a bible/draft conflict requiring a canonical decision.

Both were previously identified by the continuity checker. Neither requires structural revision -- both are fixable with minor edits.
