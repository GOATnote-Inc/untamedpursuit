## Errors (must fix)

### 1. POV Break -- Thomas-Alone Coda (lines 212-216) ✓ FIXED (2026-03-05): Replaced "She left with George" with "At the doorway she paused and looked back" — Clara now observes Thomas at the desk before closing the door and leaving. All Thomas details (maps, pen, "the pen did not move") are now Clara's direct observation through the open doorway. Hallway/Spruce Street paragraph moved to after the door closes.

- **Category**: POV discipline
- **Description**: After Clara leaves the room (line 210: "She left with George"), the narration follows Thomas sitting alone at the mahogany desk. Lines 212-216 are written from an omniscient or Thomas-limited perspective -- Clara is not present to observe Thomas, his maps, or the pen that does not move.
- **Draft says**: "Thomas sat at the mahogany desk. The maps on the walls showed coastlines from the Chesapeake to Nova Scotia -- every harbor, every approach, every anchorage annotated in his hand over four years. None of them showed the Pacific. He had never drawn the Pacific. / The pen lay on the desk where he had set it. The transition documents could begin in the morning. / The pen did not move."
- **Bible says**: The chapter's POV is Clara Chen. Third-person limited. "One POV character per chapter. Never break POV." (Root CLAUDE.md). Clara has physically left the building at this point.
- **Location**: Lines 212-216
- **Note**: The previous subagent review log (continuity-notes.md, line 1243) attempted to address this by using "external camera" language (replacing "he had memorized" with "annotated in his hand over four years"), but the fundamental issue remains: Clara is not in the room. She cannot observe Thomas sitting, the pen lying on the desk, or the pen not moving. This is a deliberate artistic choice for the chapter's emotional payoff (the Thomas jealousy thread), but it breaks the stated POV rule. The author must decide whether to (a) keep the coda and accept the POV break as an intentional exception, (b) rewrite the coda from Clara's perspective (e.g., what she imagines as she walks away), or (c) cut it.

## Warnings (review needed)

### 1. Warehouse Registry -- "Two-Floor Structure" Description

- **Category**: Internal consistency (minor)
- **Description**: Thomas says the registry shows "a two-floor structure with a third floor noted for seasonal storage." The bible and ch-06 both establish the warehouse as "three stories of Pennsylvania limestone." The language "two-floor structure with a third floor" is a plausible administrative framing (the original building was two floors, with the third added and filed later), but it could be read as contradicting the established three-story description.
- **Draft says**: "The registry shows a two-floor structure with a third floor noted for seasonal storage." (line 50)
- **Bible says**: "Warehouse: three stories of Pennsylvania limestone on Front Street" (samuel-taylor.md); "Three stories of Pennsylvania limestone, the mortar clean, the windows recently glazed" (ch-06, line 36)
- **Location**: Line 50
- **Recommendation**: This reads fine as an administrative detail (the third floor was added to the registry after the original two-floor filing), but the author may want to clarify the language slightly -- e.g., "a three-floor structure whose third-floor notice lists seasonal storage" -- to avoid any impression of contradiction.

### 2. "The Clerk from Three Weeks Ago" -- Timeline Compression

- **Category**: Timeline
- **Description**: Clara says "Wilkins. The clerk from three weeks ago." In ch-15, Wilkins visited Samuel's warehouse "yesterday afternoon" from Samuel's POV (ch-15 set in "Autumn 1846"). Ch-16 is set in "Late Autumn 1846" with November details. This implies a three-week gap between ch-15 and ch-16, which is plausible given the seasonal shift (ch-15 had no November markers; ch-16 explicitly describes November). However, Clara's phrasing "the clerk from three weeks ago" implies Clara learned about Wilkins three weeks prior. In ch-15, Samuel told Thomas about the clerk, and Thomas said he'd verify credentials. Clara was present for that conversation (she arrived later in the scene). So her knowledge of Wilkins is on-page. The three-week gap simply must be accepted as the time between chapters.
- **Location**: Line 48
- **Recommendation**: No change needed. The gap is implicit but defensible.

### 3. Thomas's Knowledge of Wallace's Pacific Ambitions -- Source Trail

- **Category**: Scene-internal knowledge
- **Description**: Thomas delivers the intelligence about Wallace's Pacific shipping contracts (line 66). This is Thomas's intelligence work -- he has sources on the southern wharves (established in ch-08) and intelligence networks (his core function). However, Thomas also references the significance of the mineral reports (line 76: "If he reaches the bay before the mineral reports become public knowledge"). The mineral intelligence was originally shared by Samuel at the Chestnut Street tea (ch-03), where Thomas was not present. Clara could plausibly have shared this with Thomas off-page at any point during their four-year partnership, and Thomas's intelligence network could independently have access to similar information (ch-03 says the mineral reports are "circulating among men of a certain standing"). This is defensible but not explicitly sourced on-page.
- **Location**: Line 76
- **Recommendation**: Consider whether Thomas's reference to "mineral reports" should be attributed (e.g., "the mineral intelligence Samuel shared" or "the intelligence from our sources") to make his knowledge source clearer. Alternatively, accept that Thomas's independent intelligence network would have access to the same whispers.

### 4. Clara's Father's Pacific Description -- Repeated with Slight Variation

- **Category**: Internal consistency (minor)
- **Description**: Clara's father's description of San Francisco harbor appears twice in the chapter -- first in Clara's interiority (line 78: "the finest natural anchorage he had ever seen") and then in dialogue to Samuel (line 124: "the most protected anchorage he had ever seen"). The adjectives differ: "finest natural" vs. "most protected." This is a minor inconsistency in how Clara remembers/reports the same recalled statement within a single chapter.
- **Draft says**: Line 78: "the finest natural anchorage he had ever seen"; Line 124: "the most protected anchorage he had ever seen"
- **Location**: Lines 78 and 124
- **Recommendation**: Harmonize the phrasing. Either "finest natural anchorage" or "most protected anchorage" should appear in both places.

### 5. Samuel's Line "He Told Us" -- Pronoun Ambiguity

- **Category**: Clarity
- **Description**: Samuel says "He told us. At the tea. The mineral surveys, the western ports. He told us what he intended and we heard him." (line 116). The "he" is ambiguous -- it could refer to Wallace or Samuel himself recalling what he told Clara. In context, Samuel is saying that Samuel himself told Clara about Wallace's intentions at the tea. But "he told us" most naturally parses as Wallace having been present at the tea, which he was not. Wallace is the one whose plans were being discussed, but Samuel was the one who delivered the intelligence.
- **Location**: Line 116
- **Recommendation**: Consider revising for clarity. E.g., "I told you. At the tea." or "Wallace showed us his hand. At the tea."

### 6. "She Did Not See What the Smile Cost Him" -- POV Boundary

- **Category**: POV discipline (borderline)
- **Description**: Line 200: "She did not see what the smile cost him. She had never thought to look." This is technically within Clara's POV as a statement of what she did NOT perceive -- a legitimate POV technique (narrating the character's blindspot). The previous subagent review (line 1248-1249) specifically addressed this as a fix for a more egregious POV breach. However, the phrase "what the smile cost him" carries implied knowledge of Thomas's interior state that Clara herself would not articulate this way. It reads more like an omniscient narrator commenting on Clara's failure to see.
- **Location**: Line 200
- **Recommendation**: This is likely an intentional artistic choice and functions well as the chapter's emotional thesis (Clara's blindspot regarding Thomas). The author should decide whether this level of implied omniscience serves the chapter's purpose. It likely does.
