## Warnings (review needed)

- **[Scene-internal knowledge / Interiority]**: ✓ FIXED (2026-03-05): Changed "ships built and broken and built again" to "ships loaded and emptied and loaded again" — corrects to dock work (loading/unloading) matching his father's established occupation. Clara's interiority at line 199 states: "She remembered his father, the dock worker. Samuel had grown up watching ships built and broken and built again." On-page, Samuel described his father as a man who "loaded other men's ships. He carried their cargo. He breathed their dust" (ch-12, line 163) and as a "dock worker" who "came to this country with nothing and built what he could with his hands and his back" (ch-06, line 104). Neither passage mentions the father or Samuel watching ships being built. The phrase "watching ships built and broken and built again" is a poetic inference Clara draws, not something Samuel told her. This is plausible as an extrapolation from knowing his father was a dock worker on the waterfront, but it introduces a specific image (shipbuilding) that goes slightly beyond the on-page facts. Author should confirm this is an intentional interpretive leap by Clara rather than an accidental factual insertion.
  - **Draft says**: "Samuel had grown up watching ships built and broken and built again"
  - **Bible says**: Samuel's father was "an Irish dock worker from Wexford" who "loaded other men's ships" and "carried their cargo"
  - **Location**: Line 199

- **[Physical detail]**: The Osprey's compartment clearance is described as "the three feet that was all a person had between the hull and the ballast stone" (line 147). In ch-12, Samuel measured the space as "less than three feet of clearance" (line 115). The difference between "three feet" and "less than three feet" is minor and could reflect different perspectives (Samuel measuring precisely vs. Clara speaking in round terms), but the chapter should be internally consistent with the established measurement.
  - **Draft says**: "the three feet" (line 147)
  - **Ch-12 says**: "less than three feet of clearance" (line 115)
  - **Location**: Line 147

- **[Ambiguity — Walking direction]**: Line 63 says "Clara and Thomas walked the two hundred yards from the commercial pier." The Osprey is described as being "at its berth in the commercial section" (line 61). The phrase "from the commercial pier" is slightly ambiguous -- it could suggest they walked from a different pier to the Osprey's location, or from one part of the commercial area to another. In ch-12, the Osprey was "two hundred yards south of Samuel's dock" (line 93). The geography is consistent (the 200-yard walk is from the area near the other ships to the Osprey), but "from the commercial pier" could be misread as the Osprey being outside the commercial section.
  - **Location**: Line 63

## New Facts Introduced

- **Inspector Hale**: Fifty years old, weathered face, careful hands, decades of inspecting cargo holds. Commission badge, leather case with measuring instruments. The compromised inspector assigned to the Osprey. Given a name here; previously identified only as "the third" (ch-13).
- **Section Fourteen**: Named provision of the harbor commission inspection protocol granting authority over cargo, manifests, and loading practices. Contains a clause about "vessel's condition as it bears on the cargo's safe transport" which Hale uses to justify structural inspection. Thomas authored this section.
- **"Structural features of undetermined purpose"**: The language Thomas suggested to Hale for the inspection record. This phrasing is now in the commission's permanent file. Wallace will have the report by end of week.
- **Osprey retired from rescue work**: The compartments are in the commission's record. The ship can never carry hidden passengers again. "The sixty-fourth would never come."
- **New rescue vessel ordered**: Clara tells Thomas to begin new specifications for a different vessel. Work begins this week.

- **Anti-trafficking campaign**: All three ships released from inspection. Ashford and Crane complaint closed as unfounded. But the Osprey's compartments are now in the commission's record -- Wallace will know the ship's purpose within the week. The rescue infrastructure has been damaged but not destroyed.
- **Thomas Arlington's jealousy seeds**: Thomas argues the Osprey's defense from inside the hull -- his most brilliant and loyal work. He shapes how the secret enters the world ("undetermined purpose" language). Clara trusts him with the most dangerous moment. He now knows every vulnerability of the compartments firsthand. On re-read, this knowledge becomes ammunition for his eventual betrayal.
- **Clara-Samuel courtship**: Clara goes to Samuel after the crisis (not Thomas), deepening the pattern established in ch-13. Samuel's response -- "Then we build what comes next" -- is forward-looking, distinguishing him from Thomas's preserving instinct. The emotional geography of Clara's life continues shifting toward Samuel's warehouse.
