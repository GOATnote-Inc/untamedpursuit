## Errors (must fix)

### 1. **[Scene-internal knowledge / spatial error]** ✓ FIXED (2026-03-05): Changed "Jennings" to "Davis" (who is in the same bungo as Harper). — Harper speaks to Jennings across separate bungos
- **Draft says**: "Boiled water only," he told Jennings for the third time that morning, watching his hand drift toward the canteen. (line 61)
- **Bible / chapter says**: The company split across two bungos (line 47): Bungo 1 has Clara, Harper, George, Davis, Marsh. Bungo 2 has Samuel, Lily, Ajax, Cole, Jennings.
- **The problem**: Harper is in Bungo 1. Jennings is in Bungo 2, twenty yards behind. Harper cannot be watching Jennings's hand "drift toward the canteen" or telling him anything "for the third time that morning" from a separate vessel on the river. This is a spatial impossibility given the established bungo assignments.
- **Location**: Line 61 (Scene 3, on the river)
- **Suggested fix**: Either (a) move the Jennings discipline beat to the camp scene (Scene 4, where everyone is together), (b) change the recipient to Davis or Marsh (who are in Bungo 1 with Harper), or (c) frame it as a shouted instruction across the water (less plausible for repeated reminders).

### 2. **[Scene-internal knowledge / character memory]**: Clara's "first seen across a counting table" reference is factually wrong ✓ FIXED (2026-03-05): Changed "first seen across a counting table" to "first seen on a dock in the gaslight." Counting table moved into the list of subsequent surfaces.

She first saw it on the docks, in gaslight, among dead men. The counting table is a later association. The counting table can remain in the list of surfaces ("a counting table, a desk, a ship's rail, a Kingston crate") without the false "first" claim. Something like: "the stillness she had first seen on a dock in the gaslight and had been seeing across surfaces ever since -- a counting table, a ship's rail, a Kingston crate."

## Warnings (review needed)

### 1. **[Character description]**: Lily described as "a girl" through Reyes's external view
- **Draft says**: Reyes sees "a girl carrying a sketchbook and a violin case" (line 39)
- **Previous chapters say**: Lily is consistently described as "a woman" -- ch-17 ("the woman holding the rifle"), ch-19 ("the clear eyes of a woman who had decided"), ch-17 ("a woman who had survived six days"). Her bible profile describes her as an adult.
- **Assessment**: This is Reyes's external perception filtered through Clara's POV narration. A stranger might describe a young woman as "a girl," especially given the cultural/temporal context. However, the narrative voice has been consistent in calling Lily a woman, and this is Clara's POV, not Reyes's. Clara would not think of Lily as "a girl." If Reyes's perspective is intended, the phrasing could be made more clearly his external assessment rather than the narrator's description.
- **Location**: Line 39

### 2. **[Continuity / Jennings injury]**: Jennings's left shoulder bandage not mentioned
- **Draft says**: Jennings appears in the bungo split (line 47) and at camp (line 81) with no mention of his wound.
- **Previous chapters say**: Jennings took a graze to his left shoulder during the pirate fight (ch-21, line 152). In ch-22 (April 17-18), he has a bandaged left shoulder (line 58). Ch-23 is April 22-24, approximately 6-7 days after the injury.
- **Assessment**: The continuity notes for ch-23 already flag this as "acceptable -- 6-7 days post-injury." A graze wound could plausibly be healed enough not to warrant mention by this point. But the rifles-across-laps detail (line 67) could be an opportunity for a brief acknowledgment if desired, since shouldering a rifle with a recently wounded left shoulder would be noticeable.
- **Location**: Lines 47, 67, 81

### 4. **[Timeline]**: "Three days out of Kingston" and the gap between chapters
- **Draft says**: "Three days out of Kingston, the water beneath the Caroline changed" (line 27)
- **Timeline**: Ch-22 is April 17-18 (Kingston). Ch-23 is April 22-24 (Chagres). The gap is 4-6 days.
- **Assessment**: "Three days out of Kingston" likely refers to three days of sailing after departure, which could mean departure on April 19 (after the two days in Kingston per ch-22) and arrival on April 22, which is consistent. This checks out. No error.

### 5. **[Scene-internal knowledge]**: Clara "had sailed the Caribbean twice with her father"
- **Draft says**: "She had sailed the Caribbean twice with her father." (line 69)
- **Bible / previous chapters say**: Clara's father traded in Kingston twice (ch-22, line 70: "Twice" in response to "Your father traded here"). Clara's father is established as a Pacific trader (Canton, Nagasaki, California). The bible says the family had "trading connections across the Pacific." Ch-21 also says she "had sailed these waters twice with her father" (line 140).
- **Assessment**: This was introduced in ch-21 and reinforced in ch-22. The claim that Clara's father traded in the Caribbean is consistent with a Pacific trader also sailing Atlantic/Caribbean routes (Canton to Philadelphia via the Cape route would pass through the Caribbean; or separate voyages). Ch-22 confirms her father traded in Kingston. This is self-consistent across ch-21, ch-22, and ch-23. No contradiction, but it expands the father's trading geography beyond the Pacific emphasis in the bible. Already noted in continuity notes.

### 6. **[POV discipline]**: Clara observes details in Bungo 2 from Bungo 1
- **Draft says**: "In the second bungo, twenty yards behind, Samuel sat with the ledger case between his feet. Lily had her sketchbook open on her knees, her pencil moving. Ajax sat beside her, ears forward, body still. Cole and Jennings watched the banks with their rifles across their laps." (line 67)
- **Assessment**: This is a common literary convention -- a trailing vessel twenty yards behind on a river could plausibly be observed by someone looking back. The details described are all visual and externally observable from that distance (sitting positions, sketchbook open, rifles across laps). This is acceptable for POV discipline but pushes the boundary; reading "her pencil moving" at twenty yards is a stretch. Consider whether this level of detail is plausible from the distance established.

## New Facts Introduced

- **Chagres settlement**: Thatched huts, ruined Spanish fort on headland (walls black with centuries of growth), single leaning wooden pier. No customs house, no clock.
- **The Caroline returns to Kingston**: Captain commands return; company committed to Isthmus crossing with no ship on the Pacific side yet.
- **Clara's shoulder rig**: Harper adapted a shoulder rig for the Colts specifically for the crossing (linen shirt, canvas trousers, shoulder rig, sword cane).
- **The guide Reyes**: Lean, sun-darkened, English learned from captains, carries a machete and nothing else. Found through Harper's "third captain" referral (the one who crossed without losing anyone, per ch-22).
- **Bungos**: Dugout canoes hollowed from single trunks, wide as a man is tall, three boatmen each, working with padded poles.
- **Company split on the river**: Bungo 1 (Clara, Harper, George, Davis, Marsh), Bungo 2 (Samuel, Lily, Ajax, Cole, Jennings).
- **River journey duration**: Three days to Cruces, then mule trail, then Panama City.
- **George in the jungle**: Overwhelmed by unfamiliar sound density; ears track without settling; unable to sort sounds as he does on the docks.
- **Caiman on the Chagres**: Slides from bank silently. Reyes: "They know the difference between a meal and a problem."
- **Camp discipline**: Green-wood fire for smoke (mosquitoes, not heat). Reyes chose elevated gravel bar with fast water. Netting strung between trees.
- **Clara on Harper**: "Not the most brilliant man she knew. The most present. The man who was there when she turned around." Four years of service.
- **Harper's forearms**: Tanned and scarred -- small marks Clara has never catalogued.
- **Correspondence code limit**: Thomas's cipher system assumes ports, post offices, ships on regular courses. The jungle offers none. First time the company exists beyond Thomas's reach.
- **Samuel writing in own journal**: Not the Thomas correspondence -- his own words, something personal.
