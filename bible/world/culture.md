# Culture & Society

<!-- Social norms, customs, class structures, religion, and daily rituals relevant to the series. -->

## Social Structure

<!-- Class hierarchy, mobility, and how characters navigate it -->

- **Clara's hidden wealth**: Operates through proxy ownership (e.g., the Golden Compass). Her true financial empire is concealed behind layers of intermediaries.
- **Samuel's self-made rise**: Son of a dock worker who built himself into a position of influence. Represents upward class mobility through intelligence and loyalty.
- **Racial discrimination**: Chinese-American experience is central. Kate is the only doctor within fifty miles of Silver Creek willing to treat both white and Chinese patients. Clara navigates prejudice through strategic concealment of her assets.
- **Irish dock workers**: Part of Philadelphia's waterfront labor force; represent another layer of ethnic tension and class dynamics.
- **Frontier egalitarianism vs. Eastern hierarchy**: The West offers more fluid social structures compared to established Eastern cities like Philadelphia.

## Religion & Belief

<!-- Dominant faiths, folk beliefs, superstitions, and their role in daily life -->

- **Clara's blended heritage**: Buddhist father (kami, harmony, gratitude, rituals) and Christian mother (prayer, grace, forgiveness, eternity). Clara draws from both traditions, creating a personal spiritual framework that bridges Eastern and Western belief.
- **The Latin motto as philosophical anchor**: "Homo sum, humani nihil a me alienum puto" ("I am human, and nothing human is alien to me" -- Terence). Functions as Clara's guiding principle and the series' thematic spine.

## Customs & Rituals

<!-- Greetings, meals, celebrations, mourning, courtship, etc. -->

- **Art and music as social ritual**: The Golden Compass serves as a cultural hub -- music hall and art gallery combined. Clara performs on multiple instruments. Hans composes. Emma creates art. These gatherings are both cultural events and expressions of the network's identity.
- **Ginger tea**: Clara's personal ritual; a quiet daily practice.
- **Wine sharing**: Red wine between Clara and Lily at Wissahickon Creek; aged red wine in Clara's study. Wine marks intimacy and trust.
- **Heritage apple cultivation**: Lily's orchard at Wissahickon Creek -- Rhode Island Greenings, Northern Spys, Baldwins. Tending the orchard is both livelihood and meditative practice.

## Gender & Family

<!-- Expectations, roles, marriage customs, inheritance -->

- **Women in medicine**: The Woman's Medical College of Pennsylvania (founded 1850) is a historical institution central to the story. Kate and Eve pursue medical careers in a profession that actively resists women's participation.
- **Women as covert power brokers**: Clara operates an empire but must do so through proxies and concealment due to the era's gender restrictions.
- **Chosen family**: The network Clara builds functions as a family structure outside traditional marriage and blood ties.

## Education & Literacy

<!-- Who learns what, and how -->

- **Medical education**: Anatomy lab study with cadavers (formaldehyde, camphor, fifty examination stations). Germ theory debated -- references to Pasteur, Koch, Lister.
- **Martial arts traditions**: Japanese swordsmanship, Chinese medicine, European fencing, and American frontier combat tactics. Training happens in the Glass Garden's courtyard (subtle lines in flagstones mark practice positions).
- **Multilingual scholarship**: Clara's study at the Glass Garden contains books in multiple languages and maps of trade routes across continents.
- **Art education**: Emma's artistic training; the Golden Compass as a space for artistic exposure and cultivation.

## Food Culture

- **Heritage apples**: Symbolic of cultivation, patience, and rootedness (Lily's orchard)
- **Ginger tea**: Clara's constant; a marker of her presence in scenes
- **Wine**: Social lubricant and marker of trust between intimate characters
