# Chapter 8: Open Water

The mast did not fight back.

Clara struck it at the third bell of the morning watch — a rising cut that landed flush against the wood and rang up through her wrist to the elbow, the impact clean and unreturned. She reset. Drew the blade back to guard. Stepped left, circled, cut again — a descending stroke this time, aimed at the spot where a man's neck met his shoulder, the junction of bone and muscle that Harper had called the hinge. The blade bit the mast and stopped. No counter. No parry. No redirect she had to account for. The wood absorbed the strike and gave nothing back, and Clara stood on the foredeck of the Resolute in the gray light before dawn and understood, in the specific language of her body, what absence meant.

Four years of morning practice. Every day since Harper took his position: the courtyard behind the Spruce Street townhouse, packed earth and flat stone, the weapons rack against the eastern wall. Harper with his own blade — shorter, heavier, the weapon of a man who preferred to absorb rather than deflect. He had never beaten her. He had known that, and she had known that, and the purpose of the practice was never victory. The purpose was the conversation. Attack and response. Feint and read. The calibration of distance, the negotiation of space, two bodies learning through repetition what each was capable of and where each was vulnerable. Four years of that conversation, and the mast offered nothing — no angle of approach, no adjustment of weight, no grunt of effort that told her she had reached him.

She struck again. The blade skidded off the curve of the wood and nearly left her hand. Sloppy. She would not have lost control with a partner — the rhythm of exchange kept the grip honest, kept the wrist aligned, kept the footwork anchored to the geometry of another body. Alone, she was practicing against furniture. The mast was the wrong shape, the wrong weight, the wrong everything. It stood where it stood and did not care whether she cut high or low, fast or slow, well or badly.

George sat on the foredeck with his head tilted to one side. Clara had seen the tilt before — it was the angle George held when he was waiting for something he expected. A sound. A signal. A man walking across a courtyard to take his position one pace behind Clara's right shoulder and stand there until the practice was done.

George waited. The man did not come.

She sheathed the blade and drew her Colts. The Colts were simpler. They did not require a partner. She aimed at the horizon — no target, no clay pots, nothing to confirm the shot. She dry-fired: five pulls on empty chambers, the clicks metered and deliberate, the sights aligned on a point where the Pacific met the sky, the distance between muzzle and target infinite and therefore meaningless. She was practicing the motion. The draw, the aim, the squeeze. The physical vocabulary of violence reviewed and rehearsed against an empty morning.

She holstered the Colts and stood for a moment with her hands at her sides. The Resolute rose and fell on a long swell — the Pacific's pulse, slower and deeper than the Atlantic's, a rhythm that suggested vast distances beneath the hull. The sky was lightening in the east. The horizon was empty — no land, no birds, nothing to suggest that anything existed beyond the circle of water they occupied. She had not seen land in three days.

---

Open water.

The phrase meant something different to Clara than it meant to Captain Morales or his crew. To Morales, open water was navigational — a stretch of ocean without hazards, without coast, without the complications that shoreline introduced. He sat on the quarterdeck rail with his clay pipe held but unlit, squinting at the horizon with the economical attention of a man who measured the world in nautical miles and wind direction. Eight knots with the current wind. Twelve days to the California coast if the wind held. Morales had said this to Samuel at breakfast, with the certainty of known quantities — speed, distance, the seasonal behavior of Pacific winds — calculations that had margins but not doubts.

To Clara, open water meant exposure. The ocean in every direction was a room with no walls, a building with its enclosures removed, a structure reduced to floor and ceiling with nothing between them to break a line of sight or provide cover. She could see the horizon in every direction, which meant anything on the horizon could see her. The openness that Morales read as safety — no reefs, no shallows, no lee shore — Clara read as vulnerability. There was nowhere to go. The ship was the only solid thing in a thousand miles of water, and the ship could not be repositioned, could not be flanked, could not be held — no men at the doors, no George at the stairs.

She walked the perimeter anyway. The circuit she had inherited from Harper and made her own — thirty paces starboard, thirty paces port, her boots on the wet deck, George's nails clicking beside her. The routine had settled into her body during the first watches after Panama, and now it continued by its own momentum — a clock still running after the hand that wound it was gone. She checked what there was to check: the rigging, the hatch covers, the companionway door, the crew on watch, the line of the horizon. She checked the position of every person on the ship relative to every other person, the geometry of a room that happened to be floating and whose walls were waves.

The company had found its shape.

The rhythms had stopped colliding. In the first days out of Panama, the ship's routine and the company's routine had grated against each other — meal times that didn't align, watch schedules that put Clara's people in the crew's path, the simple friction of twenty-four souls on a vessel built for eighteen and their cargo. Morales had handled it with indifference — he had carried difficult passengers before. He did not police the overlap. He let the two systems — his crew's and Clara's — find their accommodation, and they had.

Reyes took the second watch — 6 to 8 AM — relieving Clara. He arrived on deck precisely, without greeting, and took his position at the port rail with his rifle laid across his forearms. He did not walk the perimeter. He stood still, scanning, covering the angles Clara's circuit could not hold while she was on the opposite side. It was not Harper's method. Harper had walked. Reyes stood. The effect was close enough — someone watching, someone positioned — though Reyes's stillness left the stern unwatched during the seconds his attention tracked the bow. Clara noted the gap. Harper would not have left it.

Cole had stopped cleaning the rifles obsessively. In the first days he had disassembled and reassembled the company's arms twice each day — a nervous habit, his hands needing occupation they could find only in the maintenance of a diminished armory. Now he cleaned once, in the early afternoon, and spent the rest of his time on the foredeck staring at the water — vacant, still, his capacity for vigilance exhausted. Clara let him rest. Exhausted soldiers made errors. She had learned that from Harper, who had learned it from Florida, who had carried it as one of the few pieces of that war worth keeping.

Jennings favored his left shoulder. The pirate's graze from the Caribbean layered beneath the cholera's weakness, the body carrying its history in the stiffness of a joint that would not fully extend. Clara had examined it on the third day out — prodding the muscle with two fingers, testing the range of motion, noting the flinch Jennings produced and immediately suppressed. Nothing torn. Nothing broken. Inflammation and fatigue in a body that had been asked to do too much for too long. She prescribed rest, which Jennings accepted in silence, and a rotation to the least demanding watch, which Jennings resisted until Clara told him it was an order and not a suggestion.

---

Lily painted at the stern.

Every afternoon, when the light shifted from the white glare of noon to the longer gold of the hours before sunset, Lily settled on the coil of rope she had claimed as her station and opened the leather satchel that smelled of turpentine and linseed oil. Ajax lay at her feet — the dog who had divided the ship with George, taking the stern and the artist and the coiled rope as his territory while George held the quarterdeck and the perimeter and Clara.

Clara watched Lily from the helm rail. Not watching — that was the wrong word, too deliberate, too purposeful. Registering. The way one registered a load-bearing wall in a room: not by staring at it but by being aware of its position, its function, the weight it held. Lily at the stern was structural. She held the afternoon in place. She gave the hours between noon and sunset a shape they would not otherwise have had, because without Lily's painting the afternoon was featureless time on featureless water, and with it the afternoon was a frame — light organized, color named, the Pacific translated into pigment and paper by a woman whose steadiness had become a fact Clara relied on without examining.

Lily was painting the water itself. Not a sunset — they would come later, Clara knew, because Lily had painted every sunset since Panama and showed no sign of stopping. This afternoon she was painting the surface of the ocean at a given hour, at a precise angle of light, with a rigor that Clara recognized as systematic. Lily was not capturing a moment. She was building a record. Each painting was a measurement — this blue at this hour, this quality of light at this latitude — and the measurements accumulated into a catalogue of the Pacific that Lily carried the way Samuel carried a manifest: as evidence, as inventory, as the comprehensive account of what the world had offered.

Samuel read below decks. Clara knew because she had checked — the cabin door ajar, the single lamp lit, Samuel on the narrow bunk with a book balanced on his chest and the provisions manifest folded on the floor beside him. He had finished counting. The arithmetic that had sustained him through Panama — water rations, quinine stores, the daily calculation of sufficiency — had been completed, and the numbers satisfied him, and he had turned to other things. He was reading the only book Captain Morales had offered to lend: a Spanish edition of something Clara had not examined closely. Samuel read patiently, learning the language through the text rather than despite it, his pencil marking words he did not know in the margins.

The three of them orbited. Clara walked the perimeter. Lily painted at the stern. Samuel read below. Their paths crossed at meals and at dusk and in the brief exchanges that served as the currency of daily life on a small ship — "The wind's shifted." "Morales says twelve days." "Lily, would you bring the wine?" — and the crossings were pleasant and unremarkable, a rhythm Clara had no architecture for — three people sharing a space not because they chose it but because the space was all there was, and the sharing required no defense.

She trained with the blade again the next morning.

The same routine: third bell, foredeck, the mast standing where it stood. But this time Clara adjusted. She had spent the night thinking about it — not consciously, not strategically, but the body rehearsing solutions while the mind rested. She moved her feet first. Changed the stance from the paired geometry she had practiced with Harper — the open stance that created space for a partner's blade — to something narrower, more centered, the feet beneath the shoulders instead of staggered. She was not waiting for a response. She did not need to protect the channel between them. She needed only to cut and recover, cut and recover, the blade moving and returning without reference to another body's position.

It was worse.

The narrow stance was efficient but lonely. The footwork of a person practicing alone was the footwork of a person who had no one to practice with. Clara struck the mast — high, center, low, the three targets she had trained on since Canton — and each strike landed and each strike stopped, and where the return should have been there was nothing. A partner's blade coming back. A weight against her steel. The physical pressure of another person's intention transmitted through four feet of metal and felt in the bones of her hand.

George had not moved from his position. The same tilt. The same waiting. A foredeck with one person on it where there should have been two.

She sheathed the blade. Her right hand ached — the web between thumb and forefinger swollen where the grip had pressed against the same callus for forty minutes without the variations that a partner's unpredictable attacks would have forced. She flexed the hand. The stiffness spread. A new kind of injury: not a wound but a pattern, the body learning the shape of its new circumstances and marking the adjustment in bruised tissue and reluctant tendons.

She drew her Colts. The swollen hand shifted the balance — she adjusted her grip, one more accommodation to absence. Five dry pulls on empty chambers. Click. Click. Click. The horizon as target, the Pacific as backstop, the distance between her and anything worth shooting at measured in days and miles and the emptiness of a room that was never built.

She holstered the Colts and walked to the stern.

"Your paint smells like the orchard."

Lily looked up from the canvas she had stretched on a frame she'd built from a broken crate and sailcloth. The Pacific was behind her — or rather, the painting of the Pacific was in front of her and the Pacific itself was behind her, and the distance between the two was the subject of Lily's ongoing argument with accuracy, which Lily appeared to be winning.

"Linseed oil," Lily said. "The same oil I use on the apple wood frames at Wissahickon Creek. The paint remembers where it came from."

"Does the paint remember, or do you?"

Lily considered this with the seriousness she gave most questions — not solemn, not heavy, but attentive, the way she was attentive to light. "Both," she said. "But the paint is more honest about it."

Clara sat on the capstan beside Lily's station. Ajax raised his head, assessed her, lowered it. The dog's approval was quiet and complete — he had decided long ago that Clara belonged in Lily's space, and the decision stood without review.

"You've painted every sunset since Panama," Clara said.

"Yes."

"And now you're painting the water at noon."

"The water at noon is harder." Lily touched a brush to the canvas — a stroke so small Clara barely saw the motion, the kind of correction that existed in the gap between intention and result. "Sunsets perform. They give you color, drama, the whole production. Noon water keeps its secrets. The light comes from directly above and the surface shows you nothing — no shadows, no depth, just reflection. You have to paint what you know is there, not what you see."

"That sounds like something else entirely."

Lily smiled. The smile Clara had learned to read over four years and two oceans — the smile that meant Lily had heard the second meaning and was deciding whether to pursue it. "Painting is always something else entirely. That's why it's useful."

Clara looked at the canvas. The noon Pacific rendered in blues she could not name — not sky blue, not Navy, not the blue of the Atlantic she had grown up beside. A blue that existed only in paint, mixed from pigments that Lily had carried from Philadelphia in tubes packed with the same care Samuel gave his manifest. The painting was not finished. The surface was layered and unresolved, the water suggested rather than depicted, and Clara found she preferred it this way — the painting in progress rather than complete, the reaching rather than the arrival.

"We'll see land in nine or ten days," Clara said. "Morales says the coast announces itself — birds first, then the color of the water changes, then the smell."

"I want to paint that," Lily said. "The moment the water changes color. The boundary between open ocean and the approach."

"I'll tell you when."

Lily nodded and returned to her canvas. Clara sat for a moment longer — the capstan beneath her, the stern rail behind, the Pacific stretching to the horizon where it met the sky in a line that Lily would probably paint tomorrow, and the next day, and every day until the water changed color and the coast appeared and the featureless ocean became a destination.

That evening, Clara stood in her cabin and looked at the leather coat.

She had not removed it. Not since Panama City — not since the room above the cantina where Harper died, where she had worn it through the surgery and the night conversation and the burial and the departure and every day and every watch since. The coat had been on her body for eleven days. It smelled of salt and old sweat and the particular chemical odor of leather that has absorbed moisture and dried and absorbed moisture again, the material cycling through the Pacific's humidity without rest.

She knew what it was. The coat was not tactical. The coat was the last thing she had worn while Harper was alive, and removing it would mean occupying a version of herself that existed entirely in the time after his death, a version that had no continuity with the woman who had walked beside his litter on the Cruces trail, and Clara was not ready to be discontinuous.

The coat was heavy. The weight had settled into her shoulders and her back and the muscles between the shoulder blades that bore the load of the leather and the holsters and the steel beneath. She felt the weight most in the evening, when the day's work was done and the watch was over and the body was allowed to register what it had been carrying. The weight compounded daily, each day's grief laid on the last — not additional mass but accumulated weight, the leather holding what the body could not put down.

She reached for the top button. Her fingers found it — brass, cold, the second button from the collar that Harper had once noted hung loose and needed tightening. She had not tightened it. She pressed the button between her thumb and forefinger and felt the resistance of the buttonhole, the small mechanical refusal that stood between wearing and removing, between continuity and the clean break of putting it down.

She did not unbutton the coat.

She stood in the cabin with her hand on the button and George at her feet and the ship moving beneath her. The coat would come off when it was ready to come off, and she was not the one who would decide when. The grief had its own schedule. She had learned this from patients at the Canton hospital — Dr. Murray pressing his thumb against a healing wound, testing the tissue, saying in his broad Scots vowels, "The body decides. Not you, not me, not the patient's will. The body decides."

Clara removed her hand from the button. She sat on the bunk and unlaced her boots — one concession to the body's need for relief that she permitted herself each night — and placed them beside the bunk where she could reach them in the dark. George settled on the floor. His head on his paws. His eyes on the door.
