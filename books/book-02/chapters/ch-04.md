# Chapter 4: The Fever

The water killed them before the bullets could finish.

Samuel understood this the way he understood any market collapse — by watching the ledger change. One morning the streets of Panama City held the ordinary traffic of a transit port: mule drivers, shipping agents, missionaries, the restless population of a place that existed so other places could be reached. By the next, the ledger's columns had shifted. The living column shrank. The dead column grew. And between them, in the margin where the accountant notes what cannot be classified, the sick accumulated — lying against walls, crouching in doorways, filling the cantinas not with commerce but with the sound of bodies expelling what they could no longer hold.

Cholera. Samuel had read about it in Philadelphia — a merchant's education included the diseases that closed ports and delayed cargo. He knew the arithmetic: contaminated water, crowded quarters, tropical heat. The incubation swift, the symptoms brutal, the survival rate dependent on clean water and luck and, in this century, not much else.

He did not know what it looked like until Panama City taught him.

The first morning after the burial, he walked to the harbor to count their provisions. The canvas bags in the room behind the cantina: rice, salt pork, coffee, the dried fruit Lily had packed at Wissahickon Creek. The water casks, three of them, refilled from the roof cistern Clara had found. The quinine — a small bottle, purchased in Kingston, enough for perhaps six doses. He counted as he had always counted — by weight, by volume, by the arithmetic of what they had against what they needed, the distance to the harbor multiplied by the number of mouths, the whole equation balanced on the assumption that healthy people consume less than sick ones.

He was no longer balancing a ledger for profit. He was balancing one for survival, and the margins were thinner than any he had worked in Philadelphia.

Clara was in the street.

He found her at the corner of the harbor road, kneeling beside a man Samuel did not know — a mule driver, perhaps, or a shipping clerk, someone whose occupation had ceased to matter the moment the cramps seized him. Clara had her sleeves rolled above her elbows. The oilcloth kit was open beside her. George stood at the entrance to the alley, not guarding Clara — guarding the approach, his body angled to block the narrow gap between the buildings, his head level and still.

Clara was giving the man water. Clean water, from the cistern — their cistern, their supply, the water Samuel had counted an hour ago. She was giving it to a stranger.

Samuel watched from the end of the street and did not interrupt.

She worked with a concentration he recognized. Not the frantic energy of someone trying to outrun grief — something steadier, more structural. She checked the man's pulse. She mixed a solution of salt and water — the treatment Dr. Murray had taught her in Canton, the treatment that the textbooks called insufficient and the field called all you had. She spoke to the man in a voice Samuel could not hear from this distance but could imagine: clear, direct, the voice of a woman who had spent a night operating on someone she could not save and was now operating on someone she might.

She had changed.

Not visibly — she wore the same leather coat, the same Colts in their holsters, the same expression of controlled purpose. But she moved differently. She entered the alley and checked both ends before kneeling. She positioned George without looking at him — a hand signal, quick and flat, that Samuel recognized from the burial: the vocabulary Harper had passed to her in the room above the cantina. She scanned the street before standing, not with the careful attention of a woman who feared danger but with the practiced sweep of a man who had spent decades reading threat from terrain.

She moved like Harper.

She did not know it. Samuel could see that she did not know it — checking the roofline was Harper's habit, not hers, and she performed it with the unconscious fluency of someone who had watched a thing done a thousand times and absorbed the motion without deciding to. Harper's daughter of the eye. His discipline of the approach. His instinct to stand between the threat and the person who mattered.

Clara had not become Harper. She had become the position Harper held, and the position was reshaping her from the inside.

Samuel stood at the end of the street and counted. It was what he did. He counted the sick — three in this street, two in the next, the numbers climbing as the morning warmed and the contaminated water did its work. He counted the company — Cole and Davis in the room above the cantina, cleaning weapons. Reyes scouting the harbor. Lily in the rear courtyard, washing linen in cistern water, her artist's hands wringing cloth with a grip that had nothing to do with painting. Ward on a mat in the corner of the cantina's back room, sweating but upright, his eyes tracking the ceiling. Jennings beside him, his left shoulder still bandaged, favoring the arm when he reached for his cup.

Two of them were already sick. Samuel could see it in Ward's stillness — a man managing what his body produced, the careful economy of someone who had begun rationing his own strength. Jennings had a sheen on his face that had nothing to do with the heat and everything to do with what the heat carried.

Samuel told Clara.

She came immediately. She checked Ward first — pulse, temperature, the palpation of the abdomen that Dr. Murray had taught her to read as Samuel read a balance sheet. Her face did not change. She gave orders: quarantine. The back room of the cantina, separate from the others. Clean water only — nothing from the public supply, nothing from the street, nothing from the harbor. The roof cistern, boiled.

"How much quinine?" Samuel asked.

"Six doses. Enough for two men if I stretch it."

"And if more fall sick?"

Clara looked at him. The same eyes — dark, level, reading him as she read a room. "Then we need more quinine or fewer days in this city."

She turned back to Ward. Samuel turned back to his ledger.

The days accumulated like debts.

Samuel managed the provisions with the discipline of a man whose father had taught him that the difference between solvency and ruin was the last shilling — the one you did not spend because you might need it tomorrow. He rationed the water. He rationed the rice. He found quinine at a shipping agent's office near the harbor — purchased at four times the Kingston price, from a man who knew what disease did to markets and priced accordingly. Samuel paid without haggling. The arithmetic of survival had no room for negotiation.

He walked the streets with his pistol loaded now. He had cleaned it the morning after the burial — stripped it, oiled it, reloaded it with powder and ball from the company's stores. The Paterson sat against his hip with a weight he had not noticed before the ambush and could not stop noticing after. It was not Clara's weight — she carried weapons the way a carpenter carried tools, with the assumption that they were extensions of the body's purpose. Samuel carried his as a merchant carried a promissory note: with the awareness that it represented a transaction he might be called upon to complete.

Clara treated strangers. She went out each morning with the oilcloth kit and the cistern water and she found the sick where they lay — against walls, in doorways, behind the market stalls where the fruit rotted in the heat and the flies thickened in the afternoon. She treated them with the same precision she had used on Harper. The same hands. The same concentration. The same refusal to acknowledge what the arithmetic made plain: that the clean water she gave strangers was water she took from the company, and the company's supply was not infinite.

Samuel did not tell her to stop. He adjusted the ledger. He found a second cistern on the roof of a warehouse near the harbor, and he arranged access with the warehouse owner — a transaction, a practical exchange, the merchant's instinct applied to a problem that had nothing to do with profit. He carried the water back in a cask balanced on his shoulder, and the weight of it reminded him of something his father had carried along the Wexford docks — not water, but the same principle: you bring what is needed, and you bring it yourself, because the man who delegates the carrying does not understand the weight.

Lily helped. She moved through the days with a steadiness that surprised Samuel — not because he had underestimated her, but because the steadiness had a quality he had not expected. She washed linen. She boiled water. She sat with Ward during the night and played violin softly, the same searching melody she had played through the wall of Harper's room, and the music did not cure the cholera but it held the silence at bay, and in a city where the silence was filling with the sounds of the dying, that was its own form of medicine.

On the third day, Jennings recovered. The fever broke in the early morning, and by afternoon he was sitting up, drinking water, his left shoulder still stiff but his eyes clear. Clara checked him with a thoroughness that bordered on suspicion — as though recovery itself were a symptom she needed to diagnose before she could trust it.

On the fifth day, Ward died.

Samuel was with him. Not because Samuel had medical training or the right words or any competence that applied to a man dying of cholera in a back room in Panama City. He was there because someone should be there, and Clara was in the street with her oilcloth kit, and Lily was boiling water, and Cole and Davis were guarding the provisions, and Reyes was at the harbor. Samuel was the one who remained, and so he sat beside Ward's mat and listened to the breathing thin and stop, and he thought of Harper — not the burial, not the grave, but the moment in the hallway when he had sat against the wall with an empty pistol across his knees and waited for a sound he did not want to hear.

He had heard that sound twice now. The interval between them was five days, and the ledger was shorter by two names than it had been when they crossed the isthmus.

Samuel thought about Harper.

Not strategically — not the ambush, not the wound, not the tactical loss of a security chief on a continent that required one. He thought about the flowers.

He had been in the audience at the Golden Compass — months ago, another world, Philadelphia. Clara had just performed: cello, violin duet with Lily, Italian arias, the original composition that had made him weep. George's head rested in Samuel's lap, the great dog's warmth steady against his knees, and Harper had appeared at his shoulder — carrying a bouquet arranged with a care that spoke of specific instruction. Autumn colors, nothing ostentatious. The kind of arrangement that said someone had considered what Samuel would want to give before Samuel had thought of it himself.

"Miss Chen will receive visitors in the private gallery," Harper had said. "If you'll follow me."

Samuel had taken the flowers. He had looked at Harper. Harper's face held its usual composure, but his eyes carried something warmer — the nearest thing to approval a man of Harper's reserve would allow a stranger to see. A small gesture within a larger orchestration.

And the trail. The last morning, before the ambush — Samuel checking his pistol in the gray light, holding it at the same angle Clara held hers. Harper had looked at the pistol. Looked at Samuel's hands. And said nothing. The deliberate silence of a man who would not name what he saw because naming it would make it something examined rather than something lived. He had looked away and moved to the next mule in the line.

The flowers. The silence on the trail. Samuel understood now what he had not understood then: Harper had been transferring something. Not the watch, not the routines — those went to Clara, because Clara was the structure and the structure needed them to stand. What Harper had transferred to Samuel was simpler and harder to name. Permission, perhaps. The permission to be present. To stand in the doorway. To carry what needed carrying.

Samuel had not known he was receiving it. He knew now.

He sat in the back room of the cantina where Ward had died and he let the knowledge settle into the place where he kept the things he did not say — the place beside his mother's Sunday music and his father's hands and the weight of a water cask on his shoulder. He filed it there and closed the ledger and stood.

---

Reyes came back from the harbor on the seventh day.

He came through the cantina door with the dust of the waterfront on his boots and the expression of a man who had found what he was looking for but was not certain it was enough. Samuel had learned to read Reyes as he read a market report — the details were in the man's posture, not his words.

"A ship," Reyes said. "The Resolute. Coastal trader, rigged for the Pacific run. Captain named Morales — he's sailed the coast, Monterey to San Francisco. He'll take passengers." He paused. "The first one I found — a brig called the Santa Elena — her captain took one look at George and refused. Said he'd carried livestock and he'd carried Christians but not both at once."

"The price?"

"More than fair, less than extortion." Reyes sat down. "He wants to leave within the week. The cholera has him nervous. Every day in port is a day his crew might fall sick."

Samuel nodded. The arithmetic was immediate: passage for the company — diminished now, Clara, Samuel, Lily, Reyes, Cole, Davis, Jennings, George, Ajax, and the remaining provisions — against the price Morales would demand and the capital Clara carried. The equation was solvable. The margins were not comfortable, but margins never were on a voyage that had already cost one man to cholera and one to a musket ball on a jungle trail.

"I'll speak with Clara," Samuel said.

He found her at the cistern on the roof. She was washing her hands — the same motion he had watched in the room above the cantina while Harper lay on the table, the same care, the same refusal to hurry. George sat at the base of the ladder, watching the alley below. The sun was setting behind the Pacific, and the light caught Clara's face as she turned toward him — the face he had watched across a tea table, across a concert hall, across a continent. Thinner now. The flesh pared away by the isthmus, by the surgery, by the days of treating strangers while her own company sickened.

"Reyes found a ship," Samuel said. "The Resolute. Captain Morales. He can take us to California."

Clara dried her hands on a cloth. She looked west, toward the harbor, toward the Pacific, toward the place they were going. Her expression held nothing Samuel could read — not relief, not urgency, not the calculation he had expected. Something quieter. The expression of a woman who had been waiting for this without admitting she was waiting.

"Good," she said. "How soon?"

"Within the week."

She nodded. She looked at her hands — the hands that had held Harper's instruments, that had held a stranger's pulse, that had given the signal that brought George to her side at the graveside. Clean hands. Steady hands. Hands that had been given a man's work and had not refused it.

"Tell Reyes to negotiate the passage. You handle the terms." She paused. "You're better at the arithmetic than I am."

It was not true. Clara's arithmetic was as precise as any he had encountered — she had built an empire on it. But she was giving him the work because the work needed doing and because giving it to Samuel was an act of trust she could not name and did not need to. Harper had given her the watch. She was giving Samuel the ledger. The architecture was the same: someone must hold what needs holding, and the holding is the service.

Samuel went downstairs to find Reyes.

The harbor lights were coming on. Ships sat at anchor in the darkening water — hulls and masts and the lanterns that meant someone aboard was awake and watching. Somewhere among them, the Resolute rode at her mooring, waiting to carry them west. Samuel walked toward the harbor with his pistol at his hip and the arithmetic of survival running in his mind and the memory of flowers in brown paper filed in the place where he kept the things that mattered.

The company was smaller than it had been. The ledger was shorter. But the accounts were not closed, and the ship that would carry them was waiting, and the man who had learned to carry water was learning now to carry something heavier.

He walked toward the harbor. The Pacific was dark.
