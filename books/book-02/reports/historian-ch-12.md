## Anachronisms

### 1. "blueprint" (line 101)

- **Item**: "the person who held the blueprint was buried on a hill above Panama City"
- **Actual origin**: The literal "blueprint" (cyanotype reproduction process) was invented by Herschel in 1842, but the word "blueprint" as a noun dates to 1882. The figurative sense ("detailed plan") is attested from 1926.
- **Severity**: High -- already on the series banned vocabulary list (caught in book-02, ch-02 and ch-03).
- **Alternative**: "plan," "design," "pattern," or "map." In context: "the person who held the *pattern* was buried on a hill above Panama City" or "the person who held the *design* was buried..."

### 2. "intelligence officer" (line 121)

- **Item**: "the systematic discipline of an intelligence officer filing reports"
- **Actual origin**: The compound "intelligence officer" dates to 1880-1914 (Merriam-Webster first known use: 1914; Collins records origin 1880-85). The professionalized concept of intelligence as a military specialty emerged in the late Victorian era.
- **Severity**: Medium -- the phrase is used in Clara's interior narration as a simile, not as a title. Still anachronistic as a concept Clara would reach for in 1847.
- **Alternative**: "a clerk filing depositions," "a mapmaker recording terrain," "a surveyor cataloguing land," or simply "a woman filing reports." The military/espionage register is the problem; the concept of systematic documentation is not.

### 3. "architecture" (figurative, lines 27, 97, 103, 133)

- **Item**: "the architecture of the delivery" (line 27), "the architecture of her awareness" (line 97), "a woman's deliberate architecture" (line 103), "let the melody build its own architecture, and the architecture held" (line 133)
- **Actual origin**: "Architecture" in its literal sense dates to the 1560s. Figurative/abstract usage ("architecture of a system/idea") is harder to date precisely. Allegorical and metaphorical uses of architectural language appear in Renaissance literature (15th-16th c.), and "architectonic" (pertaining to systematization of knowledge) dates to 1801. The figurative sense is borderline for 1847 -- not impossible, but the heavy repetition (four instances in prose, plus one in YAML frontmatter) pushes it toward a modern register.
- **Severity**: Low -- this is Clara's established metaphor domain (architectural, spatial, structural). Previous historian reports have noted it as "sometimes caught" (anachronism-rulings.md). The word functions as character voice rather than narrative vocabulary.
- **Note**: The banned vocabulary list flags "architecture (figurative, for organization)" with alternatives "bones, plans." However, in this chapter the word is used for *structure* and *form* rather than for organization, which is closer to its historical range. Four occurrences in a single chapter is heavy. Consider replacing one or two with "structure," "form," or "design" to reduce the density without losing Clara's voice.

### 4. "perimeter" (military/security sense, lines 39, 55, 101)

- **Item**: "I'll need to adjust the perimeter" (Harper's remembered speech, line 39), "the perimeter was clean" (line 55), "the perimeter was secure" (line 101)
- **Actual origin**: "Perimeter" as a geometric term dates to the 15th century. The military sense ("boundary of a defended position") is attested from 1943 (OED). Even the broader military usage is dated to the 1860s at earliest.
- **Severity**: Low -- **inconsistent prior rulings**. The ch-08 historian verified "perimeter" as period-appropriate. The ch-10 historian flagged it and changed it to "border." Book 1 continuity notes show it was caught and changed to "grounds," "keep the watch," etc. Despite these corrections, the word persists across 14+ occurrences in Book 2 chapters (ch-02, ch-03, ch-06, ch-07, ch-08, ch-09, ch-12) and is deeply embedded in Harper's characterization and Clara's security vocabulary. It has effectively become an established series term.
- **Recommendation**: This needs a series-level ruling. Either (a) add "perimeter" to the banned list and sweep all remaining instances, or (b) accept it as a deliberate anachronism in Harper's/Clara's tactical vocabulary and mark it as such in the bible. The current inconsistency -- fixed in some chapters, retained in others -- is worse than either option. For this chapter specifically, line 39 is Harper's remembered speech (hardest to change without losing his voice), while lines 55 and 101 could use "watch" or "border" if the term is banned.

## Verified Accurate

- **Tin cups aboard ship**: Tin was a standard material for shipboard drinking vessels in the 18th-19th centuries. Sailors furnished themselves with tin pots, pans, and cups. Confirmed.
- **Ginger tea**: Established as Clara's preference (bible, technology-and-daily-life.md). Ginger was widely available through Pacific trade networks. Confirmed.
- **Quarterdeck**: Standard naval term for the aft section of the upper deck, used on both naval and merchant vessels. Confirmed period-appropriate for a two-masted coastal trader.
- **Companionway**: Nautical term dating to 1742 (OED). Confirmed.
- **Pine planking for cabin walls/bulkheads**: Pine was a standard shipbuilding timber, used for masts, planking, and interior construction. Three-inch planking for cabin dividers is plausible. Confirmed.
- **Pitch sealing joints**: Oakum (tarred hemp) driven into seams and sealed with hot pitch was the standard caulking method for wooden vessels. Pitch softening in warm climates is realistic. Confirmed.
- **Wine sealed with wax and cork**: Standard preservation method for wine bottles in the 19th century. Cork sealed with wax was the norm; unlabeled bottles were common (labeling was not yet standard practice). Confirmed.
- **Wissahickon Creek vineyard**: Thomas Livezey discovered grapes growing on his Wissahickon Creek property in 1767. Pennsylvania experienced a wine boom in the mid-1800s. The Alexander hybrid grape (first American vinifera-labrusca cross) was discovered near Philadelphia c. 1740. Vines "along the south wall" of a creek property producing small, thick-skinned, dark grapes is entirely plausible for a native or hybrid grape cultivar in the Philadelphia area. Confirmed.
- **Third vintage from established vines**: Lily's account of three vintages (first terrible, second less terrible, third adequate) is realistic for young vines learning their terroir. Grapevines typically produce usable fruit by year 3-4. Confirmed.
- **Violin aboard ship**: Personal instruments including violins were common aboard 19th-century sailing vessels. Lily's private playing (not sea shanties) is consistent with a passenger, not crew. Confirmed.
- **"Palette" (art)**: The word and the object date to the 1460s. Standard artist's vocabulary by the 19th century. Confirmed.
- **"Catalogued"**: The verb form dates to 1596 (OED). Well-established by 1847. Confirmed.
- **"Alchemy" (figurative)**: Figurative use of "alchemy" was well established by the 19th century (Shelley, Goethe). Confirmed.
- **"Systematic"**: Dates to 1658 (OED). Standard English by 1847. Confirmed.
- **"Surveyor" as metaphor**: The occupation and the word are medieval. Period-appropriate. Confirmed.
- **"Geometry" (figurative)**: Used figuratively in Romantic literature (Wordsworth). Period-defensible. Confirmed.

## Opportunities for Period Detail

1. **Wine bottle color**: "Dark glass" is accurate but could be enriched. Early 19th-century wine bottles were typically dark olive-green or amber, hand-blown, with pontil marks on the base. A detail like "dark green glass, the base rough where the pontil had marked it" would add period texture.

2. **Grape variety**: Lily says she doesn't know the variety. This is plausible and smart -- named cultivars were not reliably catalogued for backyard vines in the 1840s. If a variety were ever needed, the Alexander grape (Pennsylvania's signature hybrid, discovered near Philadelphia c. 1740) would be the historically accurate choice.

3. **Violin strings**: In 1847, violin strings were gut (sheep intestine), not the steel or synthetic strings used today. Gut strings are more affected by humidity and temperature changes at sea -- they would need frequent retuning. A passing reference to Lily tuning in the humidity could add maritime authenticity.
