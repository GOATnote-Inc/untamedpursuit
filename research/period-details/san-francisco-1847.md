# San Francisco in 1847 — Before the Gold Rush

## Summary

In June-July 1847, San Francisco was a tiny settlement of ~457 people on the edge of Yerba Buena Cove, five months into its new name (renamed from Yerba Buena on January 30, 1847). Under U.S. military occupation since July 1846, it was governed by an American alcalde (military mayor). The Mexican-American War was ongoing — the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo would not be signed until February 1848. The Gold Rush was 18 months away. The settlement was raw, sparse, and available.

## Population & Demographics

- **~457 inhabitants** in 1847 (most men under 40)
- Occupational census: 1 minister, 3 doctors, 3 lawyers, 1 schoolteacher
- Mixed population: American settlers, Mexican Californios, European merchants (English, Irish, Scots, French), native Californians
- Up from ~200 in mid-1846 and ~50 in 1844 — growing but still tiny

## Geography & Natural Landscape

- **Yerba Buena Cove**: the settlement's harbor, sweeping inland from Clarke's Point (below Telegraph Hill, at present Broadway/Battery) south to Rincon Point (near present Folsom/Spear). Ships anchored in this cove.
- **Montgomery Street**: ran along the cove's western edge — this was the waterfront. The bay came up to Montgomery Street. (Later filled and built over.)
- **Clarke's Point**: rocky promontory jutting into the bay below Telegraph Hill. The original landing place. Ships' boats came ashore here.
- **Telegraph Hill**: the prominent hill north of the settlement (not yet called Telegraph Hill — that name came ~1849 with the telegraph semaphore)
- **Sand dunes**: covered roughly one-third of the peninsula, stretching seven miles inland from Ocean Beach. The western side of the settlement was dune country — shifting sand, low scrub, wind.
- **Vegetation**: no planted trees. Coastal scrub, native grasses, stunted coast live oaks. Wildflowers in spring. By June-July: golden-brown dry grass on the hills. The scrub was green year-round but the grass turned gold.
- **Spring at Clay Street**: a spring of good water flowed from under the bank at the foot of Clay Street and supplied the ships. A crucial feature for any harbor settlement.

## Climate (June-July)

- **Cool**: average highs 60-70°F (15-21°C), lows 50-55°F (10-13°C). This is NOT warm — visitors from eastern cities were often surprised by how cold San Francisco summers were.
- **Fog**: characteristic summer pattern. Mornings typically overcast with coastal stratus. Clearing on the bay side by afternoon. The ocean side often stayed fogged in. Mark Twain's famous (and later) quote about SF's cold summers reflects a genuine pattern.
- **Wind**: persistent, especially afternoon westerlies pushing through the Golden Gate.
- **Dry**: no rain from May through October. Dust and sand blown by wind.

## Buildings & Infrastructure

- **~100-150 buildings** by mid-1847 (reaching ~200 by 1848)
- Construction: mix of adobe (Mexican-era), wooden frame (American-era), and shanties
- **Portsmouth Square**: the plaza — center of civic life. Named for the USS Portsmouth. Site of:
  - Mexican adobe Custom House (where the American flag was first raised July 9, 1846)
  - California's first public school (erected 1847, SW corner of plaza)
  - Religious services and public meetings held here
- **Leidesdorff's house**: on Montgomery Street, belonging to William Leidesdorff. A notable building — the hill near it was described as "impassable for wagons or horses"
- **Spear's grist mill**: two-story frame building on north side of Clay Street between Montgomery and Kearny. Mule-powered. First grist mill in California.
- **Broadway Street Wharf**: first wharf built in 1847, extending into Yerba Buena Cove from the foot of Broadway near Clarke's Point
- No gas lighting (that came later). Oil lamps and candles.

## Streets

Named by Lt. Bartlett in 1847:
- **Clay Street**: ran down to the waterfront and the spring. A primary commercial artery.
- **Montgomery Street**: along the waterfront. Would become the financial heart of the city.
- **Kearny Street**: named for General Stephen W. Kearny
- **Washington Street**: near the plaza
- **Dupont Street** (now Grant Avenue)
- **Stockton Street**
- **Market Street**: location established by Jasper O'Farrell's survey in 1847, though not yet built up

The streets were unpaved — mud in wet weather, dust and sand in dry. The hill on Montgomery near Leidesdorff's house was impassable for wagons.

## Harbor & Maritime

- **Yerba Buena Cove** was the anchorage — ships anchored offshore, cargo transferred by small boats and lighters
- Only **6 trading vessels** entered the bay in 1847 — this was a quiet port
- **Broadway Street Wharf** (1847): the first permanent wharf structure
- No harbor master's office, no customs infrastructure beyond the old Mexican Custom House
- Ships anchored within view of the settlement — the cove was intimate, not a vast harbor
- The Golden Gate (the strait, not the bridge): the passage from the Pacific into San Francisco Bay. Ships entered through this narrow gap between headlands.

## Government & Political Situation

- **U.S. military occupation** since July 9, 1846 (Captain John Montgomery, USS Portsmouth)
- **Alcalde** (military mayor): Lt. Washington Bartlett issued the renaming proclamation
- Mexican-American War ongoing — California was occupied territory, not yet formally American
- Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (Feb 1848) would cede California to the U.S.
- Military justice, not civilian courts. The alcalde combined executive and judicial functions.
- **Anti-desertion ordinance**: one of California's first labor laws — $50 reward for turning in deserting sailors

## Notable People Present in 1847

- **William Alexander Leidesdorff**: Danish-West Indian (Black heritage) merchant, ship captain, entrepreneur. Town treasurer and council member in 1847. Staged California's first horse race near Mission Dolores in 1847. Died 1848. Widely believed to be the first millionaire of African American descent.
- **Jasper O'Farrell**: Irish-born surveyor who mapped and planned the city in 1847. Corrected earlier street grids, established Market Street.
- **Sam Brannan**: Mormon leader who arrived with ~200 colonists on the Brooklyn in July 1846. Publisher of the California Star newspaper. Would become the man who announced the gold discovery in 1848.
- **Lt. Washington Bartlett**: alcalde who renamed Yerba Buena to San Francisco

## Sensory Details for Fiction

### What Clara Would See (Arriving by Ship, June 1847)
- The Golden Gate: narrow strait between headlands, fog pouring through like smoke
- San Francisco Bay opening up — vast, blue-gray, dotted with a few anchored vessels
- The settlement on the shore: a scatter of buildings — adobes and wooden frames clustered around a plaza, dwarfed by the hills behind
- Telegraph Hill rising above the settlement to the north
- Sand dunes stretching west and south — golden-brown, windblown
- The cove: ships at anchor, a single wooden wharf at Broadway
- No forest, no trees to speak of — scrub and grass and bare hills
- After Philadelphia's brick and gaslight: staggeringly sparse. Raw. Available.

### What Clara Would Smell
- Salt air and fog — wet, cold, a Pacific salt different from the Atlantic's
- Dried grass and sage from the hills
- Tar and hemp from the wharf
- Cookfire smoke — wood and dung, not coal (no coal supply yet)
- The spring at Clay Street — clean water flowing from the bank

### What Clara Would Hear
- Wind. Constant. Through the Golden Gate, across the dunes, rattling the wooden buildings
- Surf on the ocean side (audible from most of the settlement)
- Ships' bells from the cove — a few, not a forest of masts like Philadelphia
- Spanish and English spoken together — Californio and American side by side
- No church bells (the mission was at Dolores, a few miles south)
- Dogs, mules, the occasional horse

### What Clara Would Feel
- Cold. In June. The wet fog settling on skin and leather
- Wind-driven sand against exposed skin
- The ground: unpaved, sandy, muddy near the waterfront
- The scale: after the ocean's vastness, this settlement feels both tiny and limitless — the land stretches in every direction, empty, unbuilt, waiting

## Monterey (Chapter 15 Setting)

- **Capital of Alta California** under U.S. military government
- **Adobe buildings** lining Alvarado Street to the Custom House
- **Custom House**: California's sole port of entry (still standing today as a historic landmark)
- **California's First Theater**: built by English seaman Jack Swan in 1846-47 (adobe portion added 1847)
- **First brick house**: built by Gallant Duncan Dickenson, who produced his own kiln-fired bricks
- **Old Whaling Station**: built 1847 as a private home
- Mixed population: Californios, English, Irish, Scots, French, American merchants and ship captains
- A more established settlement than San Francisco — decades of Spanish/Mexican administration
- Harbor sheltered by the Monterey peninsula

## Relevance to Untamed Pursuit

**Book 2, Act III (chapters 17-26), set June-July 1847:**
- Clara arrives by ship through the Golden Gate into a settlement of 457 people — the contrast with Philadelphia is extreme
- The harbor is Yerba Buena Cove: intimate, a few ships, one wharf. Clara can see the entire settlement from the ship.
- Portsmouth Square is the center of everything — the plaza, the Custom House, the school. Clara's harbor building for the trading post would be on or near Clay/Montgomery streets.
- The fog and cold will surprise characters coming from the tropics of Panama. Clara's leather coat is now practical again, not just grief armor.
- Leidesdorff as a real-world figure: a Black millionaire operating as town treasurer. His presence establishes that the settlement, though tiny, contained people of unusual ambition and diverse origin. (Do not make him a character — just atmosphere.)
- Wallace's agents would operate through the handful of merchants and harbor contacts. In a town of 457 people, everyone knows everyone — secrecy is nearly impossible. Clara's advantage: she arrives with a company, George, and reputation. Wallace's agents have been scouting from within.
- The settlement's rawness — no paved streets, sand dunes, wind, fog — creates a physical world completely unlike Philadelphia's brick-and-gaslight sophistication.

**Book 2, Chapter 15 (Monterey):**
- More established than SF — adobe buildings, Custom House, theater
- The capital: where military authority resides
- Lily paints the coastline; Samuel walks the shore with her
- The question ("Do you trust him?") happens in a place that looks permanent — but California's permanence is Spanish, not American, and even the Spanish buildings are crumbling
