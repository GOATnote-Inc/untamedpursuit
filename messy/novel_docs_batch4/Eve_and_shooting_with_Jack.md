# Chapter: Evening Rituals

Evening light caught brass casings scattered across the practice ground. Eve had been working on a particularly complex sequence, the kind that pushed even her standards. Not every shot had found its mark.

"Off day?" Jack's voice carried no judgment, just familiar understanding.

Eve smiled slightly, collecting spent shells. "You'd think after all these years I'd stop getting frustrated by them."

"Says the woman who got that same speech from me about surgical precision last week." His dry tone made her laugh.

"You're going to have off days," they quoted together - an old refrain, shared so many times it had become almost ritual.

"You know," Eve said, examining a casing, "I gave that exact speech to Sarah yesterday. About sutures."

"The apprentice becomes the teacher." Jack's voice held quiet pride. "Though I seem to remember someone telling me the same thing about tracking during that snowstorm last winter."

Storm drifted closer, apparently deciding this familiar exchange required her presence. The mare had heard this conversation enough times to know its rhythms.

"We practice to narrow the margin on misses," Eve quoted their shared wisdom. "Mother says the same thing about diagnostic reviews."

"Smart woman, your mother." Jack collected more brass. "Though I notice she still gets frustrated on her off days too."

Eve nodded, understanding flowing between them. This was not about teaching or learning anymore - had not been for years. It was about two professionals sharing a truth they both lived with: that mastery included accepting imperfection while always striving to narrow its margins.

Storm chose that moment to lip at Eve's hair - her version of participating in their ritual.

The sun painted brass shells like scattered stars as father and daughter collected their equipment, no more words needed. Some wisdom did not need new expression - it just needed regular acknowledgment.

Like family traditions. Like shared understanding.

Like the space between perfect shots that they both learned to accept while never stopping their drive toward excellence.
