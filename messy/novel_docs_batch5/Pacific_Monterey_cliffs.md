# The Pacific Threw Itself Against Monterey's Cliffs

The Pacific threw itself against Monterey's cliffs, endless and empty as grief. Eve sat with her boots dangling over the edge, watching Queen and Storm graze behind them.

Ghost lay between them, head on paws, watching the horizon with Jack's steady patience.

Jessie? The name came quiet, almost lost in the surf.

Mm? Jessie didn't look up from cleaning her guns.

Can I tell you stories that are meaningless? Eve's voice caught slightly. Just so my family's memories don't fade into sunsets?

Jessie's hands stilled. She set the gun aside with uncharacteristic gentleness.

Tell me.

Eve watched a gull ride the updraft. Did I ever tell you about the morning Dad taught me to read air?

Read air?

That's what he called it. Ghost was just a pup... She closed her eyes, letting memory wash over her. It was about Mom, really.

The story spilled out then - morning dew and spider webs, bird patterns and Ghost's early training, the way Jack taught through silence and Kate taught through precision.

They were always teaching, Eve said softly. Every moment, every choice, shaped to show some truth I'd need later. She looked down at her hands - surgeon's hands, shooter's hands. Her mother's precision, her father's strength. I never thanked them enough for that.

They knew. Jessie's voice carried rare gentleness.

Tell me another? she asked instead.

They're fading, Eve whispered. Not the big moments. But the small ones - how Dad smelled of gun oil and sage. How Mom's accent got sharper when she was worried.

So you tell me stories. Jessie's shoulder pressed against hers. And I'll remember them with you. And someday you'll teach your own students about reading air, and their memory lives on.

Ghost lifted his head suddenly, scenting something on the wind.

Someone coming, Jessie said quietly.

Eve closed her eyes, reading air the way Jack had taught her. Three riders. One horse favoring its right front...

The ocean kept rolling in, carrying memory and loss and love on each wave.

Sometimes family was blood and memory. Sometimes it was choices and chances.

Sometimes it was friends who asked for meaningless stories, understanding they were anything but meaningless.

Sometimes that had to be enough.

Sometimes it was everything.
