# Unravelled Clara

Eve's practiced hands moved with gentle efficiency as she examined Clara, each touch revealing another piece of a puzzle she wished she wasn't solving. The morning light streaming through the conservatory's glass ceiling illuminated Clara's face, highlighting the subtle hollowing of her cheeks, the shadows beneath her eyes that makeup couldn't quite conceal.

"How long has the cough been producing blood?" Eve kept her voice steady, professional, even as her heart tightened in her chest.

Clara's laugh turned into a weak cough. "Trust you to see through my little deceptions." She dabbed at her lips with a fresh handkerchief. "A few weeks, perhaps? The mornings are worst."

Samuel paced behind them, his usual commanding presence fractured with worry. "I've tried to convince her to rest more, to eat more. She insists on maintaining her usual schedule."

"Because I refuse to become an invalid over a persistent cough," Clara said with a flash of her usual spirit. But Eve noticed how she winced slightly when shifting position, how her left arm moved stiffly.

Eve's fingers probed gently along Clara's left side, feeling what she'd feared to find. The mass was substantial, hard as a river stone and fixed in place. The skin above it showed subtle dimpling, and the nearby lymph nodes were swollen and firm. All the signs she'd been taught to dread.

"Clara," Eve said softly, "I need you to be honest with me now. What other symptoms have you been hiding?"

Clara's eyes met hers, and in that moment, the pretense fell away. "The pain... it started in my breast, but now it's in my back as well. And sometimes, when I try to take a deep breath..." She gestured vaguely at her chest.

Eve nodded, reaching for her stethoscope. The brass felt cool against her palms as she warmed it before placing it against Clara's back. She listened intently, noting the decreased breath sounds on the right side, the subtle crackle that shouldn't be there.

"You've lost weight," Eve observed, continuing her examination. "More than can be explained by reduced appetite alone."

"Has she?" Samuel stopped pacing, his face tightening with concern. "I hadn't... the dressmaker just adjusted her gowns. I thought..."

"It's remarkable what a skilled seamstress can disguise," Clara said softly. "I didn't want to worry you, my love."

Eve continued her examination, each finding adding to the weight in her chest. Her mother had taught her to be thorough, to gather all the evidence before drawing conclusions. But some patterns were impossible to ignore, especially when they aligned with everything she'd studied about this particular enemy.

"Clara," Eve said finally, setting aside her stethoscope, "I need to discuss my findings honestly with you and Samuel. Would you prefer to move somewhere more comfortable?"

Clara's hand found Samuel's, gripping it with surprising strength. "Here is fine. The flowers... they help me breathe better. And I've always believed bad news is easier to bear among beautiful things."

Eve nodded, choosing her words carefully. The medical texts were clear about the progression of this disease, but they said nothing about how to tell a dear friend that her time was measured now in months rather than years.

"The mass in your breast has spread," Eve began, her voice gentle but clear. "The cough, the chest pain, the difficulty breathing - these suggest involvement in your lungs. The back pain may indicate..." She paused, seeing understanding dawn in Clara's eyes.

"How long?" Clara's voice was steady, though her grip on Samuel's hand tightened.

Eve met her friend's eyes directly, her voice gentle but unflinching. "The cancer has spread beyond your breast, Clara. It's in your lungs, which explains the breathing difficulties and the blood. The pain in your back suggests it's reached your bones as well."

Clara nodded slowly, her expression calm. "I suspected as much. I've always appreciated your honesty, Eve." "I'm asking how long I have to put my affairs in order. To say my goodbyes. To make sure those I love are prepared."

Eve felt tears threatening but held them back. This was why her mother insisted that doctors maintain professional distance. But how could anyone remain distant when the patient was also a friend, a sister in spirit?

"A few months," Eve said softly, squeezing Clara's hand. "I'll be with you every step of the way. We'll manage the pain, help with your breathing, and keep you as comfortable as possible. I promise you won't face any of this alone."

Samuel made a sound like he'd been struck, but Clara simply nodded. "Then we'll make them good months." She turned to her husband, reaching up to touch his face. "My darling, would you ask Harrison to bring some tea? And perhaps send word to my solicitor? There are matters we should discuss while I'm feeling strong."

Samuel looked like he wanted to protest, to rage against this verdict, but years of loving Clara had taught him to respect her wishes. He bent to kiss her forehead before striding from the conservatory, his shoulders rigid with suppressed emotion.

Once he was gone, Clara turned back to Eve. "Tell me everything," she said. "No gentle evasions, no pretty lies. I need to know what to expect, how to prepare, how to make this easier for him." She gestured toward where Samuel had disappeared. "He'll need help, you know. After. Promise me you'll help him find his way through this?"

Eve took her friend's hand, feeling the subtle tremors that Clara had learned to hide so well. "I promise," she said. "Whatever you need, whatever he needs - I'll be here."

The morning sun continued to stream through the conservatory glass, painting everything in deceptively cheerful colors. Among Clara's beloved orchids and ferns, Eve began to explain the path that lay ahead, her words as precise and careful as her earlier stitches had been. But this wound, she knew, would leave scars that no amount of medical skill could heal.
