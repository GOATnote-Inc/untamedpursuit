# Chapter One: The Celebration of Life

The late afternoon sun painted the horizon in hues of gold and rose as Eve Garrett stepped out of the carriage, her boots crunching against the gravel path. She adjusted her wide-brimmed hat, its shadow offering no solace from the heaviness in her chest. Beside her, Jessie, with her trademark smirk, fidgeted with her gloves, uncharacteristically quiet.

Clara's sprawling Monterey estate loomed ahead, its once-bright white walls muted by time and salt air. A wind swept off the Pacific, carrying with it the scent of seaweed and the faintest echo of waves crashing against the rocky shore below. Eve tightened her shawl against the chill, though the cold she felt wasn't from the air.

The last time she'd visited Clara's home, it had been for training - early mornings on the sand, sparring sessions in the studio, long talks over tea that stretched into the night. But today was different. Today, Clara had gathered everyone she loved, not to mourn her, but to celebrate her. And yet, the thought of Clara - strong, indomitable Clara - slipping away felt impossible to accept.

Eve hesitated at the door, her knuckles hovering above the polished wood. For a moment, she let herself imagine Clara on the other side, seated in her favorite armchair, lost in thought as she often was these days. The illness had stolen much of her strength, but never her sharp mind or her wit.

Finally, Eve knocked, three soft raps that barely carried over the sound of the wind.

The door opened almost immediately, and there she was. Clara's frame had diminished, her shoulders frailer than Eve remembered, but her presence still filled the space like sunlight through an open window. Her graying hair was swept back neatly, and her pale skin bore the faint flush of someone who refused to surrender to weakness.

"Eve." Clara's voice was as warm as the sun, though it carried a rasp now, a reminder of the battle waging within her. She smiled, her lips trembling slightly, and reached out. Eve clasped Clara's hand gently, careful not to squeeze too hard.

"You didn't think I'd miss your party, did you?" Eve said, her attempt at lightness faltering as her throat tightened.

Clara chuckled, leading them into the house. Jessie followed silently, her usual bravado tempered by the solemnity of the moment. Inside, the familiar scent of eucalyptus and lavender wrapped around Eve like an embrace. Clara's apprentices moved quietly through the halls, arranging flowers and plates of food, their faces set with determination as though willing the evening to be a joyful one.

"I wanted to see all of you before I go," Clara said as they entered the sitting room. She sank into her armchair with a grace that belied the pain she must have felt. Eve noticed how she winced, ever so slightly, as she settled into the cushions. "It's a rare thing, you know, to have the time to say goodbye."

Eve knelt beside her, the weight of unspoken words pressing on her chest. "It's a gift," she said softly, her green eyes searching Clara's face. "But it doesn't make it easier."

Clara smiled again, her gaze distant for a moment. "No, it doesn't. But I've always believed that our time on this earth is what we make of it. Regardless of where we come from or what we believe, today is what matters. The rest is..." She waved a hand vaguely, as if dismissing the very notion of an afterlife or a tomorrow.

Eve looked down at Clara's hand, now resting on her knee, and felt a swell of gratitude for this woman who had shaped so much of who she was. "You've taught me that more than anyone else ever could."

Clara tilted her head, studying Eve with an intensity that made her feel as though she were being weighed and measured. "And you've taught me something, too, Eve. That fire of yours - it reminds me of myself at your age. But you're smarter than I ever was, and kinder, too."

Eve felt her cheeks flush. "I don't know about that."

"I do," Clara said firmly. "And I'm proud of you."

The words settled into Eve's chest, warm and heavy, as though Clara had passed some unspoken torch. Before she could respond, Jessie cleared her throat from where she stood by the mantel, fiddling with a picture frame.

"So," Jessie said, her voice unusually soft, "how do you two know each other?"

Eve glanced at Clara, who raised a brow as if to say, *Go on*. "Clara has been someone I've always looked up to," Eve began, her voice steadying. "At first, she was this untouchable figure - someone I wanted to be like but never thought I'd have the chance to know. Then, somehow, I got lucky enough to learn from her. And now..." She hesitated, glancing at Clara. "Now, I'd like to think of her as a peer."

"A peer?" Clara echoed, a spark of amusement in her eyes. "That's a generous assessment."

"It's the truth," Eve said. "You've taught me everything I know, Clara. And not just about fighting or surviving. You've taught me how to live."

Clara's expression softened, and for a moment, the room seemed to hold its breath. Then she laughed, the sound low and full of life. "Well, I suppose I've done my job, then."

The three of them fell into a comfortable silence, the kind that only comes from shared history and mutual respect. Outside, the waves continued their eternal rhythm, and the golden light of the setting sun bathed the room in a soft glow.

Today was all they had, and somehow, it was enough.
