Episode I

25 year old Clara Chen stood at the edge of her private dock, breathing in the complex mixture of tar, rope, salt, and violence. George, her massive black Continental Shepherd, sat alertly at her side, his intelligent eyes scanning the waterfront perfectly synchronizing with hers. Night had settled over Philadelphia's port, and a forest of ship masts seemed to shimmer with the star-filled sky.  The rigging creaked softly in the evening breeze, a familiar song.

Her London-tailored leather coat, cut to the latest fashion while concealing multiple Colts, moved silently with each breath of wind. When Harper approached, George's ears twitched slightly—he'd recognized the security chief's footsteps long before they reached human hearing.

"Miss Chen." Harper's voice carried quiet concern. "The carriage is ready."

Clara didn't turn, her silver-handled sword cane tapping absently against the weathered planks while George remained perfectly still beside her. To most observers, the cane and the dog appeared elegant accessories—precisely as intended. Her fingers found the grip of her favorite Colt, which she'd used that morning to outshoot Harper again: six shots, six bullseyes, all while walking.

"Mr. Harper, I am far from defenseless," she said softly, noting George's subtle shift toward movement in the shadows near the cargo stacks. A rat, nothing more.

"I've yet to meet anyone who can match your accuracy with a pistol," Harper admitted, "much less your speed with multiple weapons."

"And the sword?" Clara's slight smile held memories of countless dawn sessions before most of Philadelphia stirred.

"While I cannot argue with your expertise in any weapon and the skill of George, numbers can beat talent."

"You are not wrong." Clara turned to face him, her coat shifting to reveal glimpses of custom holsters, each weapon placed precisely where muscle memory could find it without thought. "What if I start on the northernmost aspect of this yard, and you take a team and start at the southernmost aspect of town? If we find nothing, I will start in the south tomorrow, and you will start here."

"May we walk with you?"

"No, Harper. Tonight, I wish to walk alone. The moon is beautiful. The water lapping is speaking to me." Her hand unconsciously checked the specialized percussion caps she'd modified for quieter firing. "I also wish to be a young woman and artist tonight. I acknowledge the risks of walking alone."

"Yes, Miss Chen." Harper's resignation carried years of watching her outshoot every security expert he'd hired. "My men and I will leave for the South docks immediately."

Clara began her walk, boots clicking softly against the cobblestones. George moved like a shadow beside her, his massive form blending into the darkness despite his size. The night held that peculiar stillness between the evening's last business and dawn's first deliveries. Most of Philadelphia's respectable citizens were home now, leaving the port to those who preferred darkness.

Her darkness wore a more elegant costume. George's thick black coat might appear handsome to casual observers, but it concealed lethal capability like Clara's silk dress. Let them think of him simply as a companion dog. Like everything in Clara's world, the truth was far more profound.

Each step placed her near potential cover while maintaining clear lines of sight. The sword cane's rhythm masks the subtle sound of perfectly balanced revolvers. This morning's practice focused on transitioning between blade and firearm, a skill that had already saved her life twice.

A scream shattered the night.

George's muscles tensed beneath his thick coat—Clara's only warning before the sound reached her ears. Not the usual drunken bellow of sailors, but something far worse: a child's raw terror.

She moved like a jaguar, George pacing beside her without command. Years of training took over. The screams came again, closer now, accompanied by the sound of struggle. At Clara's subtle hand signal, George split away, circling to approach from another angle—a tactic they'd practiced until it became instinct.

Tonight, they would defend those who could not defend themselves.

**Episode 1 Act II**

“Just one more night," Samuel Taylor muttered, watching the Mercy Jane's crew from a hidden position near his newly purchased warehouse. Three weeks of surveillance had confirmed his suspicions—the ship's legitimate cargo operation masked something darker. Tonight, they'd finally made a mistake.

Children's whimpering, followed by one terrified scream, carried across the docks.

"Hurry up\!" A rough voice hissed. "Get them to the cart before—"

Sam moved. "Let them go," he called out, stepping into the moonlight. His hand rested on his pistol, but he kept his tone conversational. "Whatever you're being paid, I'll double it."

Six men. More than expected. The closest one spat. "Mr. Taylor. Told the captain you were asking too many questions."

"Last chance," Sam said quietly. "Release the children."

The first attack came from behind—an amateur mistake. Sam sidestepped, using the man's momentum to send him sprawling. But the others rushed him together, and a wooden plank caught his gun hand with numbing force. His pistol clattered away.

Blood sprayed as the same plank connected with his jaw. His training took over—he blocked, struck, and kept moving. But they had even more numbers, and the children's terrified screams tore at his concentration.

A flash of movement caught his eye—a woman in an elegant coat, moving with lethal grace. Before he could process this development, a massive black dog materialized from the shadows like an avenging spirit. The Continental Shepherd's attack was devastatingly efficient. One moment, the children's captors were dragging their struggling prey. The next, they were on the ground, the dog's powerful jaws ending their criminal careers with surgical precision.

Sam barely had time to admire the dog's technique before the woman went into action. Her sword cane transformed into a lethal blade with liquid grace, removing his immediate attacker's hand in a spray of arterial blood. Her left hand produced a Colt with impossible speed, and two more traffickers fell to precisely placed shots.

The suppressed reports of her modified percussion caps impressed him almost as much as her accuracy. Military training, indeed, but something more... Her movements spoke of weapons experts with thousands of hours of dedicated practice.  She had dropped an empty Colt, and a new Colt was half-spent before the other hit the ground. 

His head was still ringing as he retrieved a criminal’s dropped pistol and was attempting to assist further in the fight. None of ten or more of the Mercy Jane’s crew was moving as he scanned for targets, and the dog immediately identified him as a new threat. The Continental Shepherd's low growl promised swift violence. 

"George, hold," the woman commanded softly. The dog stilled instantly, though its eyes never left Sam.

She moved with fluid grace, replacing her half-spent Colt with a fresh one even as she scanned for threats. "Hi there. I’m Clara Chen," she said, as casually as if they were meeting at a social function rather than standing among cooling corpses.

"Samuel Taylor," he managed, tasting blood from his split lip and loose teeth. Her bearing stirred memories of rumored military operations—the kind of lore or tall tells. "That was remarkable shooting."

Her smile held at his compliment seemed to pause time. “Thanks.” She said.

The dog—George—had positioned himself protectively near the children, his massive form offering safety and comfort. Sam watched with fascination as the terrified young ones instinctively sought shelter against his thick coat.

A man emerged from the shadows, taking in the carnage with professional detachment. "Miss Chen. Mr. Taylor." His tone suggested he'd seen similar scenes before. "Shall I arrange cleanup?"

"Yes," Clara confirmed. And ensure the children's families understand they'll be under our protection going forward." She nodded toward where George stood guard. "George will assist in escorting them home."

Sam studied her with growing interest. Everything about her suggested layers of complexity—the perfectly tailored coat concealing multiple weapons, this elegant sword cane transformed into a lethal blade, now cane again, the exceptionally trained dog that moved as an extension of her will. Even her calm efficiency in the aftermath of violence spoke of extensive experience.

"You have questions," she observed, those remarkable eyes assessing him with equal interest.

"Several thousand," he admitted. 

"Tea tomorrow?" Her smile held a hint of challenge. "Three o'clock at this tea house," she said while handing him her card. 

Sam touched his swollen jaw carefully. "You just saved my life and the life of these two girls. For this I am eternally in your debt. You also just killed 12 or more men in what seemed to be a blur.  You just commanded a renowned weapons master Mr David Harper.  Miss Chen, I am surely dead if I am not at tea tomorrow.”

“Great, I am looking forward to our tea Mr. Taylor. please excuse my abrupt departure.” She said an immediately disappeared into a carriage. 

Am I dreaming? Sam thought as his head was still ringing, but then he looked at her card which was his new most prized possession.

ACT III: The Aftermath

Thomas's office occupied the ground floor of Clara's townhouse—a space that balanced understated elegance with practical efficiency. Fine Persian rugs softened footsteps while tactical maps lined the walls, each carefully selected to appear decorative to casual observers. George lay near the marble fireplace, his alert presence adding another layer of security to their midnight meeting.

"How did the Mercy Jane get past our guard?" Clara asked, standing at the window. Her reflection ghosted against the glass, overlaying the gaslit street beyond.

Thomas looked up from his desk—a magnificent piece of mahogany that had once graced a governor's office. "Manifests." He spread several documents across the polished surface. "Legitimate cargo layered with illegitimate. Unfortunately, this story is common and the bane of our efforts."

George's ears twitched at approaching footsteps, then relaxed as he recognized Harper's gait.

"What suggestions do you have that may stem this tide?" Clara's fingers traced the silver handle of her sword cane.

"Short term?" Thomas leaned back, lamplight catching the silver at his temples. "Tighten ship inspections. Levy a tax that requires inspectors to see all cargo. Create law that hangs perpetrators." His expression hardened. "Long term? End poverty. Educate all regarding the dangers. Make sure people no longer view slavery as acceptable."

Clara moved to join him at the desk, George rising smoothly to pad alongside her. "What are we doing to fund these efforts?"

"I don't know of anyone doing more." Thomas's voice softened with old affection. "You seem to be giving away the fortune of many kings to achieve these goals." He straightened some papers. "It's not enough."

"I have two tasks for you to consider." Clara settled into her favorite chair, George taking his customary position at her feet. "First, fix this. Lay out your best plan to help us achieve these short-term and long-term goals. Create a list of the top ten things we can do to institute these changes. Then, name the most important thing I can help with on that list. I want to focus on that for the next week."

"And the second task?"

"By our weapons practice in the early afternoon, I want everything you can find out about Samuel Taylor. I have tea with him at 3 pm."

Thomas's eyebrows rose slightly as he studied his oldest friend. "Professionally? Consider it done." A smile tugged at his lips. "Personally... Sam Taylor is handsome as hell." He leaned forward. "Tea? Okay. What are you wearing? What are you serving? Where? Tell me."

Clara laughed—a rare, genuine sound that made George's tail thump once against the carpet. "I love you, Thomas."

"And well, you should." He began gathering papers with practiced efficiency. "Now, about Mr. Taylor's background—some interesting patterns exist in his property acquisitions. Rather like someone else I know..."

George's head lifted suddenly toward the window. A moment later, distant shouting carried from the direction of the docks.

Clara was already moving. "Have that information ready by morning practice," she called over her shoulder. "George, with me."

Thomas watched them go, shaking his head with fond exasperation. Another conversation was interrupted by Clara's endless war against darkness. But as he returned to his research, his smile held pride. Few knew the true scope of her influence—how many lives she'd saved, how many children she'd protected, how many fortunes she'd spent in pursuit of justice.

The grandfather clock struck midnight as Thomas began his investigation into Samuel Taylor. Tomorrow promised to be very interesting indeed.
