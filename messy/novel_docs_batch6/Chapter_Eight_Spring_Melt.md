# Chapter Eight: Spring Melt

The Snake River ran angry in April. Winter death filled its banks with snowmelt and fury, turning the familiar ford into a treacherous gamble.

Ghost reached the edge first, hackles rising. The big mare halted beside him, studying the current patterns. Queen stood slightly back.

We could ride north, Jessie said. Hit the bridge at Taylor Creek.

Ten hour detour. Eve checked the medical bag waterproofing. Anderson wife will not last that long.

The big mare took her first step down the bank without being asked. Each hoof placement deliberate, testing. Ghost swam beside them, his grey coat dark with water.

Queen and Jessie followed, staying slightly right of their path.

Halfway across, the shot cracked against stone.

Eve felt her mare muscles bunch a heartbeat before the second round kicked water three feet left. They moved as one diving right. The current grabbed them, but the big mare found purchase against the riverbed.

Queen and Jessie were already returning fire, the black mare standing steady despite rushing water. Ghost found the far bank in three powerful strokes, taking high ground.

Eve drew her rifle one-handed. The river fought them with every stride, but the horse never lost footing.

Another shot closer. Eve spotted the muzzle flash and squeezed her trigger. A yell carried over the water roar. Then Jessie rifle spoke and a body tumbled down the rocks.

Ghost bark cut sharp warning of flankers downstream. The big mare responded without guidance, angling toward better cover.

Three more shots, then silence.

They reached the far bank as two riders broke from cover fleeing south. Let them run, Jessie said. They will spread the word.

They had six miles to Anderson ranch. His wife fever would not wait.

Eve checked her medical supplies somehow still dry. Ghost took point following game trails. Behind them the river ran on, indifferent to the violence it had witnessed.

The mares picked up pace. Ghost ranged ahead and behind checking their flanks.

Eve felt her mother medical bag, her father Winchester. Felt the sure stride beneath her. The steady presence of Jessie and Queen beside them.

The river lesson remained: survival was about trust. Partnership. Knowing who had your back when the current tried to pull you under.

Spring storms were just beginning. More rivers waited ahead.

They rode on.
