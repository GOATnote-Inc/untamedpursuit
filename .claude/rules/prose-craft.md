# Prose Craft — Always Active

This rule has no path scope and is always loaded. Structure keeps a story standing; prose craft makes it breathe.

## Curiosity

The narrator — filtered through the POV character — should notice what's *interesting*, not just what's plot-relevant. Let characters be fascinated, puzzled, delighted by the world around them. A character who only registers what matters to the plot feels like a camera. A character who notices the odd detail, the unexplained habit, the texture of a stranger's coat feels alive.

## Wit

Humor lives in timing, understatement, irony, and the gap between expectation and reality. Every character's humor is distinct:

- **Clara**: Dark gallows wit — finds the grim joke in terrible situations
- **Samuel**: Self-deprecating candor — undercuts his own authority before anyone else can
- **Kate**: Bone-dry precision — says the devastating thing without raising her voice
- **Jessie**: Survival humor — laughs to keep moving, jokes as armor

Wit is never decorative. It reveals character, relieves tension purposefully, or sharpens a scene's edge. If a joke could belong to anyone, it belongs to no one.

## Imagination

Surprise the reader. Metaphors should connect unexpected domains — not "her heart sank" but something only *this* character, in *this* moment, would think. Descriptions should find the one detail that makes a scene irreplaceably specific: not "a busy market" but the detail that makes it *this* market on *this* day.

Avoid the first image that comes to mind. It came to mind first because it's been used before.

## Sentence Craft

Vary rhythm. Short sentences for impact. Longer sentences for immersion, for the feeling of time stretching, for drawing the reader deeper into a moment before the snap of something short.

Read sentences aloud mentally — they should have music. Watch for accidental meter that creates a singsong effect, and for strings of identically structured clauses.

The strongest sentences do two things at once: advance plot *and* reveal character, describe setting *and* establish mood, deliver information *and* create tension. Single-duty sentences should be the exception, not the norm.

## Subtext

What characters don't say matters as much as what they say. Dialogue should have a surface meaning and an undercurrent. The reader should sometimes understand more than the characters do.

Actions should betray what words conceal — the hand that tightens on a cup, the pause before a too-casual answer, the subject that keeps not being mentioned. Trust the reader to read between lines.

## Character Metaphor Domains

Each POV character thinks in metaphors drawn from their lived experience. When writing or revising, ensure metaphors are POV-specific:

- **Clara**: Architectural, spatial, tactical. She thinks in structures, sight lines, load-bearing walls, blueprints, the geometry of rooms and combat. Her world is built and defended.
- **Samuel**: Commercial, mercantile, inventory. He thinks in ledgers, cargo, balance sheets, manifests, the arithmetic of value and cost. His world is counted and weighed.
- **Thomas**: Legal, linguistic, textual. He thinks in arguments, precedents, drafts, the weight of specific words. His world is written and filed.
- **Harper**: Military, positional, tactical-minimal. He thinks in sight lines, formations, terrain, the economy of force. His world is watched and guarded.
- **Lily**: Natural, artistic, sensory. She thinks in color, growth, seasons, the textures of materials. Her world is observed and rendered.

If a metaphor could belong to any character, it belongs to none. Replace generic images ("living thing," "smile that survived everything," "had not changed her") with domain-specific ones that only this character, seeing through this lens, would produce.

## Voice Under Pressure

Characters' speech and thought patterns shift under emotional stress. Stress strips away performance and reveals the core: formal characters become blunt, verbose characters go quiet, careful characters get reckless.

Specific per-character stress responses belong in their bible profiles and will be added incrementally as characters appear in drafts. The principle is universal: pressure changes voice, and those changes are where character is most visible.
